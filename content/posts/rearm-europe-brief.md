## The Funding Landscape

The European Commission's ReArm Europe programme represents the most significant shift in EU defence funding since the Common Security and Defence Policy was established. Three instruments are being shaped simultaneously during Ireland's Presidency term:

**SAFE (Security Action for Europe).** A €150 billion loan facility to support member state defence investment. The eligibility criteria and preferential terms are being negotiated now. If SAFE defines eligible investment as major weapons platforms — tanks, fighters, frigates — Ireland's contribution domains are excluded from preferential financing.

**EDIP (European Defence Industrial Programme).** A €1.5 billion grant programme for joint defence procurement and industrial cooperation. The implementing rules will determine which forms of defence cooperation qualify. If EDIP defines participation as defence manufacturing output, Ireland's technology sector — which hosts the EU headquarters of most major US tech firms — is excluded.

**MFF Defence Chapter.** The next Multiannual Financial Framework will include, for the first time, a dedicated defence chapter. The framework text being drafted now will determine priority domains for the next seven years of EU defence spending.

## What Ireland Should Secure

Ireland's objective across all three instruments is the same: ensure that domain-specific contribution — maritime surveillance, subsea infrastructure protection, cyber resilience — is recognised alongside traditional military capability as a qualifying form of European defence contribution.

Specifically, Ireland should seek:

- **SAFE Phase 2 preferential terms** for investments in maritime surveillance technology, subsea cable monitoring, and cybersecurity infrastructure.
- **EDIP implementing rules** that define Ireland's technology sector hosting as a form of defence-industrial contribution.
- **MFF framework text** that names maritime, subsea, and cyber as priority investment domains alongside traditional capability categories.

## The Presidency Advantage

Ireland does not need to be the loudest voice in the room. The Presidency controls the agenda, the timeline, and the compromise text. Ireland can propose language, set the order of discussion, and shape the text that other member states negotiate around. This procedural authority is the mechanism through which Ireland's interests are advanced — not through spending weight or military capability.

## The Risk of Inaction

If Ireland chairs these negotiations without shaping the rules, the default outcome favours states with large defence-industrial bases. France, Germany, Italy, and Spain will write rules that recognise their forms of contribution. Ireland's domains — surveillance, infrastructure protection, cyber — will be treated as secondary unless Ireland acts during its six months in the chair.
