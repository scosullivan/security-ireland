## Why Now: The Collapse of the Free-Riding Equilibrium

Ireland's security free-riding on the United Kingdom was a stable equilibrium because the costs were low, the benefits were high, and no actor had sufficient incentive to disrupt it. The UK provided security for the shared domain as a byproduct of its own defence posture, and Ireland received that security without acknowledgment. The arrangement was invisible enough to avoid triggering the neutrality debate on the Irish side and low-cost enough to avoid triggering resentment on the UK side.

Three developments are disrupting this equilibrium simultaneously. The threat environment has become material and visible — the Russian vessel Yantar was observed deploying a deep-sea mini-submarine off the Irish coast in 2023, was escorted from the Irish Sea in November 2024 after patrolling above subsea cables, and during President Zelensky's December 2025 visit, Portugal's GNR provided drone defence near Leinster House because Ireland could not.

The EU is forcing the question. EU defence spending rose from €218 billion in 2021 to an expected €381 billion in 2025. Ireland, at 0.2 per cent of GDP, is the lowest spender in the Union and assumes the Council Presidency in July 2026. The UK has changed its calculus — at the Cork summit on 13 March 2026, the two governments signed a refreshed Defence MoU, a Subsea Infrastructure Framework, committed to live exercises in September 2026, and Ireland applied to the North Sea Joint Declaration. The UK is no longer content to provide security silently.

## The Shared Security Domain

The physical geography of the island creates security interdependencies that exist regardless of neutrality, NATO membership, or constitutional positions. They arise from infrastructure, physics, and network topology.

![The Shared Security Domain](/images/charts/uksi_shared_domain.png)

**Maritime.** Ireland's Exclusive Economic Zone covers approximately 880,000 square kilometres of the northeast Atlantic. Russian submarine activity has increased since 2014. The Naval Service operates with no functioning main armament and can deploy one large vessel at any time. It has no anti-submarine capability. The UK Royal Navy provides the de facto maritime umbrella with no treaty basis.

**Airspace.** Ireland has no primary radar, no fighter aircraft, no surface-to-air missiles. Under a longstanding arrangement, the RAF intercepts potentially hostile aircraft in Irish airspace, sometimes without prior Irish awareness. The airspace over the island is operationally continuous — a radar gap over Ireland degrades situational awareness for the entire island.

**Subsea infrastructure.** Over twenty major cables make landfall on Irish shores, carrying a significant proportion of EU–US data traffic. The Celtic Interconnector to France arrives by 2027. The Far North Fiber cable linking Ireland, Alaska, Japan, and Norway is planned for 2026. The Nord Stream sabotage demonstrated that subsea infrastructure is a domain of active hybrid threat.

**Energy.** The island's energy grid operates as a coupled system through the Single Electricity Market. Disruption propagates cross-border in real time. Energy security is an island-wide condition, not a jurisdictional one.

**Cyber and hybrid.** Ireland hosts the European headquarters of most major American technology companies. The 2021 HSE ransomware attack cost hundreds of millions. Ireland's NCSC remains under-resourced and its intelligence architecture is fragmented across three departments.

## The Architectural Asymmetry

![Defence Spending Comparison](/images/charts/uksi_spending_comparison.png)

The UK maintains a professional military of approximately 145,000 active personnel, global intelligence capabilities, a blue-water navy, and full-spectrum air force. Ireland's institutional system has been architecturally configured for the absence of military capability over the course of a century — not a gap to be filled but the actual design of the state, tracing back to the Free State government's effective disbandment of the National Army in 1923.

Ireland's €42 billion budget surplus between 2022 and 2024 is proof that resources are not the constraint. The constraint is institutional bandwidth: the distance between what the system can currently coordinate and what the shared security domain requires. The asymmetry means the security interface is structurally one-sided: the UK provides the umbrella without governance, accountability, or Irish capacity to reciprocate.

## The EU Dimension: Complements, Not Competitors

A serious objection to structured UK–Ireland bilateral security cooperation is that it may compete with EU defence integration. This objection deserves a direct answer: the tracks are complements, not competitors.

![Ireland's Layered Security Cooperation](/images/charts/uksi_cooperation_tracks.png)

Ireland joined PESCO in 2017 and has participated in seven projects as a full member. The projects it chose are revealing: Upgrade of Maritime Surveillance, Maritime Semi-Autonomous Systems for Mine Countermeasures, Cyber Threats and Incident Response, Critical Seabed Infrastructure Protection, and Network of Logistic Hubs. These map directly onto the Cork MoU's focus areas. Maritime surveillance capability developed through bilateral cooperation feeds into the EU's Common Information Sharing Environment. The bilateral track builds institutional capacity that makes the EU track credible.

Ireland's Council Presidency from July 2026 transforms the EU dimension from context to active constraint. The layered cooperation approach provides the narrative Ireland needs: contributing to European security through structured cooperation in the domains where its geography makes it critical, using bilateral, EU, and NATO-adjacent partnerships that generate real capability without compromising its constitutional position.

## Working Within the Neutrality Constraint

Nothing in this framework requires Ireland to abandon neutrality. The relevant distinction is not between neutrality and military alliance, but between coherent and incoherent management of a shared security domain that already exists. Ireland's subsea cables are already at risk; its airspace is already being protected by a foreign air force; its energy grid is already coupled to the UK's. The question is not whether Ireland participates in shared security — because it already does. The question is whether that participation is structured, governed, and resilient, or ad hoc, unacknowledged, and fragile.

Defence Minister McEntee's formulation captures it precisely: "We're militarily neutral, but we're not neutral to any of the threats." Neutrality governs Ireland's military alliance posture. It does not exempt Ireland from managing the security of shared infrastructure, shared waters, and shared airspace.

## The Three-Layer Architecture

![The Three-Layer Security Architecture](/images/charts/uksi_three_layers.png)

The emerging security cooperation framework operates across three distinct institutional layers.

**Governance layer: state-to-state and multilateral.** The Cork MoU, the Subsea Infrastructure Framework, the North Sea Joint Declaration, PESCO projects, and NATO's ITPP all operate at this layer. It is correctly located at the state-to-state level. The Cork summit has established the governance architecture; the task is to deepen it.

**Interface layer: Northern Ireland as strategic node.** Northern Ireland's post-Brexit position at the boundary between UK and EU regimes gives it positional value that is geographic and regulatory. It is where UK maritime assets in the Irish Sea operate, where energy interconnectors cross, and where the regulatory boundary creates coordination demands.

**Implementation layer: the gap.** The Cork MoU creates commitments at the governance layer, but the operational consequences play out on the island: subsea cables land on coasts in both jurisdictions, the energy grid is coupled across the border, maritime domain awareness involves assets and agencies on both sides. There is no mechanism for coordinating island-level implementation. This is the gap the companion paper addresses.

## Stabilisation Mechanisms

The shared domain requires four tiers of stabilisation. **Tier 1: Friction monitoring** — continuous monitoring of maritime incident response times, airspace detection latency, subsea infrastructure status. **Tier 2: Trusted operational routines** — named contacts, regular information-sharing cycles, pre-agreed edge-case procedures. **Tier 3: Shared incident response protocols** — common classification, notification sequences, shared situational awareness tools; the September 2026 live exercises are a Tier 3 mechanism. **Tier 4: Interoperability governance** — equivalence mapping between UK and Irish security standards, ensuring cooperation remains functional as systems evolve independently.

## Implementation Pathway

**Phase 1: Foundation (months 0–12).** The Cork summit has begun this phase. Deepen the MoU commitments: map the shared domain in operational detail, establish named contacts, commission a joint friction audit before the September exercises. Integrate the bilateral track with EU commitments.

**Phase 2: Routinisation (months 12–24).** Activate standing meetings, regular information-sharing cycles, agreed edge-case procedures. Expand exercises to maritime, cyber, and energy domains. Prepare for primary radar data-sharing (target 2028).

**Phase 3: Integration (months 24–36).** Formalise shared protocols across all domains. Establish interoperability governance. Integrate radar data as Irish primary radar becomes operational. Assess what institutional arrangements the implementation layer requires.

The governance is set. The interface position exists. The gap is operational — and filling that gap is the subject of the companion paper.
