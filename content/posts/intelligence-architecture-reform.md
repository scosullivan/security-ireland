## The Current System: Three Silos

Ireland's intelligence architecture is not merely under-resourced; it is structurally fragmented in a way that no other European state replicates. Three agencies, each under a different government department, perform intelligence functions with no unified analytical capability, no joint tasking authority, and no shared intelligence platform.

![Ireland's Current Intelligence Architecture](/images/charts/iar_current_architecture.png)

**The Irish Military Intelligence Service (IMIS)**, renamed from J2 in July 2025, is the intelligence branch of the Defence Forces. It operates domestic and foreign intelligence sections, providing intelligence concerning threats to the security of the state. IMIS reports to the Department of Defence. The Defence Forces Intelligence School was formalised in 2025, establishing a professional pathway for military intelligence careers. Its budget and personnel numbers are classified; the only publicly known funding is the €2 million Secret Service budget shared with the Garda.

**The Garda National Crime and Security Intelligence Service (GNCSIS)**, formerly the Crime and Security Branch, is both a security and organised crime intelligence service with a large operational arm, including firearms operations and counter-terrorism investigations. It reports to the Department of Justice. The Commission on the Future of Policing (2018) called for a ring-fenced budget and the ability to recruit specialist expertise directly — recommendations yet to be fully implemented. A new multi-storey, high-security facility for the GNCSIS at Garda HQ is planned but expected to take five years to build.

**The National Cyber Security Centre (NCSC)** reports to the Department of Communications. It is responsible for cyber threat detection, incident response, and the transposition of the EU's NIS2 Directive into Irish law. Ireland is subject to European Commission infringement proceedings for late NIS2 transposition.

The **National Security Committee (NSC)** is the inter-departmental committee chaired by the Secretary General to the Government. It provides coordination but has no operational authority, no unified tasking capability, and no statutory mandate. The National Security Analysis Centre was quietly dissolved in early 2025 and now operates as a secretariat within the Department of the Taoiseach. It has yet to publish Ireland's first National Security Strategy, initially expected in 2021.

The fragmentation is not a gap to be filled. It is the design of the system.

## Why the Fragmentation Is a Binding Constraint

The fragmentation is not merely an organisational inconvenience — it is a binding constraint on every other security capability Ireland is trying to build.

Maritime surveillance requires the fusion of naval patrol data, satellite imagery, AIS tracking, and signals intelligence into a coherent operational picture. When Ireland's seven PESCO projects generate data — from maritime surveillance, subsea infrastructure monitoring, and cyber threat detection — that data must be analysed, fused with bilateral intelligence from the Cork MoU, and converted into actionable assessments. A fragmented intelligence system cannot perform this fusion. IMIS handles military intelligence, the NCSC handles cyber, and the Garda handles internal security — but no agency has the mandate or the capability to produce the integrated threat assessments that the shared security domain requires.

The Cork MoU's cyber cooperation track establishes NCSC-to-NCSC threat intelligence sharing with the UK. But bilateral cyber intelligence must also inform IMIS assessments of hybrid threats and Garda assessments of critical infrastructure risk. In the current architecture, this requires manual coordination between agencies under different departments — a process that is slow, incomplete, and dependent on personal relationships rather than institutional protocols.

The December 2025 drone incident during President Zelensky's visit illustrated the problem in real time. Unidentified drones were detected seventeen kilometres offshore. Portugal's GNR provided drone defence near Leinster House because Ireland could not. The incident involved maritime surveillance (Naval Service), airspace detection (Air Corps, which has no radar), law enforcement (Garda, which operates the only counter-drone system), and cyber assessment (NCSC). Four agencies under three departments, with no unified command or intelligence fusion capability, could not coordinate a response to protect the capital during a high-profile state visit.

The Commission on the Defence Forces (2022) was explicit. It recommended the development of an overarching legal and governance framework to ensure optimum coordination between the Defence Forces and An Garda Síochána on intelligence matters. The Government's implementation plan put these recommendations down for "further evaluation" — originally due by June 2024, now pushed to December 2027.

## The Target Architecture

The target is not a single monolithic intelligence agency on the MI5 or DGSI model. Ireland's scale, constitutional position, and institutional culture make that neither desirable nor achievable. The target is a coordinated architecture in which existing agencies retain operational independence but operate under unified strategic direction, joint tasking, and a shared analytical platform.

![Target Intelligence Architecture](/images/charts/iar_target_architecture.png)

**National Security and Intelligence Agency (NSIA).** A new statutory body with a legislative mandate to provide unified intelligence analysis, joint tasking, and a shared intelligence platform. The NSIA would not absorb IMIS, GNCSIS, or the NCSC; it would sit above them as the integrating layer — receiving raw and processed intelligence from all three agencies, producing fused threat assessments, and tasking collection priorities based on a unified threat picture. The model follows the Dutch approach (where AIVD and MIVD retain independence but share a joint SIGINT organisation, the NSO) and the Finnish approach (where Supo reports to the Ministry of the Interior but operates under PM Office coordination).

**National Security Council (elevated).** The current NSC should be elevated to a Cabinet-level committee with statutory authority, chaired by the Taoiseach or a designated senior minister. The Programme for Government has committed to establishing a "new Cabinet level committee on national security." This is the governance layer — setting strategic priorities, receiving integrated threat assessments from the NSIA, and making resource allocation decisions.

**Shared intelligence platform.** A classified, integrated information system that allows all three agencies to share intelligence at appropriate classification levels. This is the technical infrastructure that makes the coordination architecture functional. The new GNCSIS facility at Garda HQ provides an opportunity to build this platform from the outset — but only if the specification includes interoperability with IMIS and NCSC systems, which requires a design decision now, not after the building is complete.

**Judicial oversight.** Any expansion of intelligence coordination requires proportionate oversight. The legislation establishing the NSIA should include a statutory Intelligence and Security Committee of the Oireachtas, modelled on the UK's Intelligence and Security Committee of Parliament, with security-cleared members and access to classified material.

## European Comparison

![European Intelligence Structures](/images/charts/iar_european_comparison.png)

Every comparable European state has integrated or coordinated intelligence under a single authority. The United Kingdom operates a Joint Intelligence Committee under the Cabinet Office. France coordinates through the CNRLT under the President. The Netherlands maintains two agencies (AIVD and MIVD) but coordinates them through a shared SIGINT organisation. Finland, Sweden, and Denmark all operate unified or tightly coordinated structures. Ireland is the only EU member state where intelligence operates across three departments with no unified structure. This is not a matter of scale — Denmark and Finland are comparable in population and face similar threat profiles.

## The Legislative Pathway

Intelligence reform requires primary legislation. The current agencies operate under a patchwork of legal authorities: IMIS under Defence Forces regulations, the Garda under the Garda Síochána Act 2005, and the NCSC under communications legislation. There is no overarching intelligence statute.

A National Security and Intelligence Act should establish the NSIA as a statutory body, define its mandate, specify its relationship to the three operational agencies, create the oversight framework, and provide the legal basis for inter-agency intelligence sharing that currently operates on informal arrangements. The Act should also clarify the legal mandate for IMIS, which the Commission on Defence Forces identified as an urgent requirement.

Surveillance reform must accompany the intelligence statute. The Interception of Postal Packets and Telecommunications Messages Act 1993 is over thirty years old and predates the internet, social media, encrypted communications, and cloud computing. The legal framework for intelligence surveillance is obsolete.

NIS2 transposition is a parallel requirement. The National Cybersecurity Bill should be expedited and designed to interoperate with the NSIA's threat assessment function — so that cyber threat intelligence flows into the unified analytical platform rather than remaining siloed in the NCSC.

## The Phased Transition

![Phased Transition to Integrated Intelligence](/images/charts/iar_phased_transition.png)

**Phase 1: Coordination (2026–2027).** This phase requires no legislation and can begin immediately. Elevate the National Security Committee to a Cabinet-level committee as committed in the Programme for Government. Appoint a National Security Coordinator with cross-departmental authority. Establish a joint tasking protocol between IMIS, GNCSIS, and NCSC. Deploy a shared secure communications platform. Commission the national security structures review promised in the Programme for Government.

**Phase 2: Legislation (2027–2028).** Draft and enact the National Security and Intelligence Act establishing the NSIA, the oversight framework, the legal mandate for IMIS, and modernised surveillance provisions. Complete NIS2 transposition through the National Cybersecurity Bill, designed for interoperability with the NSIA. Establish the Oireachtas Intelligence and Security Committee. Begin NSIA recruitment — drawing on the existing talent pool across the three agencies and recruiting externally for specialist analytical, technical, and legal expertise.

**Phase 3: Integration (2028–2031).** The NSIA becomes operational. The unified analysis centre produces integrated threat assessments for the National Security Council. The shared intelligence platform connects IMIS, GNCSIS, and NCSC data streams. Bilateral intelligence sharing with the UK and multilateral sharing through PESCO and EU mechanisms flows through the unified platform. The primary radar programme (target 2028) feeds airspace data into the same system. For the first time, Ireland has a single intelligence picture covering maritime, airspace, subsea, energy, and cyber domains.

## Risks and Constraints

**Political resistance.** Intelligence reform touches deep institutional sensitivities. The Defence Forces, An Garda Síochána, and the NCSC each have institutional cultures, ministerial relationships, and resource claims that a unified structure would disrupt. The NSIA must be designed as a coordination layer, not a replacement.

**Civil liberties.** Any expansion of intelligence coordination raises legitimate concerns about state surveillance. The legislative package must include robust safeguards: judicial authorisation for surveillance, an Inspector General for intelligence, the Oireachtas Committee, and EU-compliant data protection. Intelligence reform without proportionate oversight would be both politically unachievable and constitutionally indefensible.

**Capacity.** Ireland's intelligence agencies are small. Building a new integrating layer requires analytical talent that is in short supply. The phased approach accommodates these constraints.

**The manifesto gap.** The standalone NSIA was proposed in the Fianna Fáil election manifesto but did not make it into the Programme for Government. If the promised review recommends something less than the full NSIA architecture — a coordinator without statutory authority, a shared platform without joint tasking — the reform will be insufficient.

## The Prerequisite for Everything Else

Intelligence reform is the prerequisite for everything else Ireland is trying to build in the security domain. Without unified intelligence, the Cork MoU's bilateral cooperation lacks an analytical backbone. Without a shared platform, PESCO project data cannot be fused with bilateral and national intelligence. Without integrated threat assessment, the shared security domain — maritime, airspace, subsea, energy, cyber — cannot be monitored as a single system.

The Commission on Defence Forces identified the problem in 2022. The Programme for Government committed to a review in 2025. The Presidency creates the political space for reform in 2026. The legislative window is 2027–2028. If that window closes without primary legislation establishing a unified intelligence architecture, Ireland will enter the next decade with the same fragmented system that could not coordinate a drone response during a presidential visit.
