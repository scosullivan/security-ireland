## In Brief

Europe is spending more on defence than at any point since the Cold War, suggesting that the political will is present and that the financial commitments are finally real. And yet the strategic objective — European defence autonomy — is receding. The more Europe spends through its current structures, the more dependent it becomes on American platforms, American supply chains, and American political decisions.

Understanding why requires distinguishing between two things that look similar but are economically distinct: defence spending and defence capability.

The relationship between them is not automatic. It depends on the existence of industrial, supply-chain, and institutional architectures that convert expenditure into capability. When those architectures are absent, spending does not become capability. It becomes dependency.

---

## The Procurement Flow Problem

When a European country increases its defence budget and procures equipment, the economic and strategic outcome depends entirely on where that procurement flows.

If it flows to European producers, it builds European industrial capacity, develops European workforce skills, funds European R&D, and creates the production architecture that makes future procurement cheaper and faster. This is the virtuous cycle that defence economists describe.

If it flows to American producers, the effect is inverted: it builds American industrial capacity, funds American R&D, and deepens European dependence on supply chains that are outside European control.

Recent data indicate that an increasing share of European defence procurement has been flowing to American contractors. This is not a political preference but a reflection of industrial reality: American contractors can deliver proven platforms faster and at lower cost than fragmented European alternatives.

![The Procurement Flow Problem](/images/charts/edb_procurement_flow.png)

This dependency now has three dimensions that are beyond political dispute.

First, US defence production is under documented strain. Backlogs are acknowledged even by analysts aligned with the current administration — and European orders would be deprioritised behind American military needs.

Second, American weapons systems increasingly incorporate remote-disable capabilities. A European military dependent on American platforms is not, in any precise sense, autonomous.

Third, the current US administration has explicitly framed European economic activity as a target of competitive pressure. A security architecture that depends on a country that regards you as an adversary in trade is not a security architecture at all.

---

## The Fragmentation Problem

The external dependency problem compounds a separate structural failure: the fragmentation of European defence procurement across twenty-seven national systems.

The EU has twenty-seven member states, each with its own defence ministry, procurement process, and industrial policy. The result is a market that is, by any industrial logic, incoherent. European defence firms face twenty-seven different customers, twenty-seven different specification processes, and twenty-seven different political considerations.

This fragmentation produces compounding inefficiencies. It prevents European producers from achieving the production volumes that would make them cost-competitive with American alternatives. It prevents standardisation. It prevents the kind of integrated supply-chain development that American contractors take for granted. And it makes every European procurement decision more expensive and slower than it needs to be.

![The Fragmentation Trap](/images/charts/edb_fragmentation_trap.png)

The result is a structural trap. European producers cannot compete on cost or delivery against American contractors because they cannot reach scale, and they cannot reach scale because European procurement is fragmented. The more Europe spends without building its own industrial architecture, the more entrenched its dependency becomes.

---

## Two Failure Modes, One Structural Cause

The external dependency and internal fragmentation problems share a common structural origin: the absence of co-evolved industrial, market, and institutional architectures for European defence.

This pattern has a precise analogue in innovation economics. When financial capital and political demand exist for a technology, but the surrounding industrial and institutional architectures have not been built, the capital flows to wherever those architectures already exist. In the case of European defence, that means the United States.

European defence is exhibiting exactly this dynamic. The political capital for rearmament is real. The financial commitments are substantial. But the industrial architecture needed to convert those commitments into European capability has not been built. So the money flows — rationally, predictably — to American contractors who already have the architecture.

This is not an argument against European defence spending. Rather, it is an argument about sequencing. Spending in the absence of industrial architecture is not neutral. It actively forecloses the development of the architecture it replaces.

---

## What an Architecture-First Approach Requires

An architecture-first approach to European rearmament would require four structural investments that are not currently being prioritised.

**European procurement coordination.** Creating the institutional machinery to aggregate European defence demand into a single market signal large enough to justify capital investment in European production capacity.

**Directed industrial investment.** Allocating capital specifically to building European production capacity in key platform and munitions categories. Not procurement subsidies for existing equipment, but investment in the facilities, tooling, and supply chains needed for European-scale production.

**Interoperability standards.** Accelerating the development and adoption of common technical standards across European armed forces — so that equipment produced in one member state can integrate with allied forces without bespoke adaptation.

**Workforce development.** Building the technical and operational workforce pipelines that make expanded defence production and capability sustainable over time. No industrial architecture functions without the human capital to operate it.

![The Architecture-First Approach](/images/charts/edb_architecture_first.png)

None of these are spending targets. But all of them are harder to implement than increasing national defence budgets. All require coordination across national governments in ways that are institutionally and politically difficult.

---

## Two Trajectories

The question European governments face is not whether to spend more on defence — that decision has already been made, and it is the correct one. The question is whether to spend in ways that build the architecture for European autonomy, or in ways that deepen European dependency while appearing to address the security deficit.

Ireland chairs EU defence budget discussions during its Council Presidency from July 2026. The coordination architecture argument — that Europe's problem is not whether it spends enough but whether twenty-seven procurement systems can convert spending into capability — is one that a non-aligned chair might make more credibly than a major arms-producing state.

Spending more through unreformed procurement channels is not a path to autonomy. It is the path away from it.
