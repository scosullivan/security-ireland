Ireland’s Layered Security



Cooperation Model



How bilateral, EU, and NATO-adjacent cooperation tracks compound rather than compete — the institutional mechanics, the friction points, and the conditions under which this model is replicable for non-aligned EU member states.



Security Ireland	June 2026



T H E   A R G U M E N T



The model



Figure 1: Three concentric tracks. EU frameworks (inner), bilateral UK–Ireland (middle), NATO-adjacent (outer). Each feeds capability into the others. The bilateral track builds institutional depth that makes EU participation credible rather than symbolic.



Ireland’s security cooperation operates across three simultaneous tracks. The EU track includes seven PESCO projects as full member (Maritime Surveillance Upgrade, Maritime Mine Countermeasures, Critical Seabed Infrastructure Protection, Cyber Threats and Incident Response, Network of Logistic Hubs, plus two further projects), CISE membership for maritime data sharing since April 2025, and approximately €350 million committed to the European Peace Facility. The bilateral track includes the Cork MoU signed March 2026 covering maritime, cyber, air domain, subsea, exercises, procurement, and training; the Subsea Infrastructure Framework for cable incident coordination; and joint exercises committed for September 2026. The NATO-adjacent track includes the Individually Tailored Partnership Programme joined in 2024, the North Sea Joint Declaration application of March 2026, and a working relationship with the UK Joint Expeditionary Force.



The analytical claim is not simply that these tracks coexist. It is that they compound. Capability built through bilateral cooperation with the UK feeds directly into Ireland’s EU framework participation. This feeding mechanism is specific and traceable, not metaphorical. Understanding how it works — and where it breaks down — is essential to making the model sustainable.



The institutional mechanics of capability transfer



Maritime surveillance: from bilateral patrols to CISE data



The Cork MoU’s maritime track establishes naval liaison and coordinated patrol operations between Irish and Royal Navy vessels in the shared maritime approach. When Irish vessels conduct joint patrols with UK assets, the surveillance data generated — vessel tracking, anomaly detection, subsurface acoustic data — feeds into the Irish Defence Forces’ operational picture. Since April 2025, Ireland has been a CISE participant, meaning this data can be shared into the EU’s Common Information Sharing Environment for maritime surveillance.



The transfer is not automatic. It passes through several institutional filters. First, the classification constraint: data generated from joint UK patrols may carry UK classification markings that are not automatically shareable into EU systems. The UK–EU security of information agreement governs what can be shared, but the practical protocols for downgrading or sanitising UK-origin maritime data for CISE ingestion do not yet exist. This is a solvable problem, but it requires explicit protocol development — something the September 2026 exercises should test. Second, the format constraint: UK maritime surveillance systems use different data standards than CISE’s interoperability framework. Data reformatting is a technical task, not an institutional one, but it requires dedicated integration capability within the Irish Defence Forces that does not currently exist at scale.



Subsea infrastructure: from bilateral framework to PESCO project



The Subsea Infrastructure Framework, a companion to the Cork MoU, establishes coordinated response protocols for cable incidents in waters shared by Ireland and the UK. When a cable is damaged or threatened, the framework specifies which agencies are notified, what response assets are available, and how jurisdictional handoffs work between Irish and British waters. This operational protocol directly strengthens Ireland’s contribution to the PESCO Critical Seabed Infrastructure Protection project, which is developing a layered protection system for European subsea cables.



The friction point is governance asymmetry. The PESCO project operates under EU governance with reporting obligations to the EDA and EEAS. The bilateral Subsea Framework operates under the Cork MoU’s bilateral governance. When an incident occurs in waters that are simultaneously covered by both frameworks, which governance takes precedence? The answer must be operational, not political: the nearest capable asset responds first, and governance alignment happens after the incident. But this operational logic must be codified in advance, not improvised during a crisis.



Cyber: from bilateral threat sharing to PESCO platform



The Cork MoU’s cyber cooperation track establishes NCSC-to-NCSC threat intelligence sharing between Ireland’s National Cyber Security Centre and the UK’s equivalent. Threat indicators, malware signatures, and incident response lessons flow bilaterally in close to real time. This bilateral intelligence strengthens Ireland’s contribution to the PESCO Cyber Threats and Incident Response project, which operates a shared threat intelligence platform among participating member states.



The classification friction is most acute in the cyber domain. UK-origin cyber threat intelligence is often classified at levels that require specific bilateral handling agreements. Sharing this intelligence into a PESCO platform that includes twenty-six member states requires either declassification, sanitisation, or parallel reporting — where the existence of a threat is shared without the specific UK-origin indicators. Ireland’s NCSC must develop the institutional capacity to manage this dual-track reporting: bilaterally sharing detailed indicators with the UK while sharing processed analysis with PESCO partners. This requires both technical systems and personnel with appropriate clearances for both channels.



The Cork MoU: anatomy and gaps



Figure 2: The Cork MoU’s seven cooperation domains, each mapping to a PESCO project or EU framework. The bilateral architecture was deliberately designed to complement EU commitments — but three operational gaps remain.



The Cork MoU covers seven domains: maritime, cyber defence, air domain information sharing, subsea infrastructure, joint exercises, joint procurement, and personnel/training exchanges. The design is deliberate: every domain maps to an existing PESCO project or EU framework. Maritime maps to PESCO Maritime Surveillance Upgrade and CISE. Cyber maps to PESCO Cyber Threats. Subsea maps to PESCO Critical Seabed Infrastructure Protection and the North Sea Joint Declaration. This alignment was not accidental — Ireland’s PESCO project selections were made precisely because they address the shared UK–Ireland security domain.



But the MoU has three operational gaps that must be closed. First, the air domain gap: the MoU commits to ‘air domain information sharing’ but does not address the elephant in the room — the secret RAF air intercept arrangement under which British fighters respond to unidentified aircraft in Irish airspace. The MoU formalises information sharing but not the operational dependency. Until Ireland has primary radar (target 2028) and some form of air response capability, the bilateral air domain cooperation is structurally asymmetric: the UK provides the capability, Ireland provides the airspace. The September 2026 exercises should test how air domain information flows when Ireland has no independent detection capability.



Second, the implementation gap: the MoU creates governance commitments at the London–Dublin level but has no mechanism for coordinating island-level operations. Subsea cables land on coasts in both jurisdictions. The energy grid is coupled across the border through SONI and EirGrid. Maritime domain awareness in the Irish Sea involves assets from Cork, Belfast, and Plymouth. Who coordinates the cross-border dimensions? This is the subject of the companion paper — The GFA and the Security Gap — which identifies bounded implementation functions that can be carried by existing cross-border mechanisms.



Third, the procurement gap: the MoU commits to ‘exploring joint procurement’ but does not specify which platforms, under what terms, or through which frameworks. Joint procurement with a non-EU state requires careful alignment with EU procurement rules — particularly if Ireland simultaneously participates in EDIP consortia that have EU co-funding. The rules are compatible but the institutional protocols for managing dual-track procurement do not yet exist.

The Austrian cautionary tale



Austria provides both a parallel case and a warning. Like Ireland, Austria is an EU member, constitutionally neutral, and not a NATO member. Austria participates in PESCO with four projects: a cyber security programme with Greece, a disaster-response programme led by Italy, a military transport initiative, and the German-led centre for training missions. Austria also participates in EU battlegroups and maintains a framework nation relationship with Germany.



But Austria’s approach to PESCO has been defensive, not strategic. As the European Council on Foreign Relations has observed, Austrian political elites — regardless of political orientation — have been primarily interested in engaging with EU defence structures to limit their ambitions, ensuring Austria is at the table to block escalation rather than to build capability. Austria joined PESCO eagerly, but chose undemanding projects and contributed minimally. The result is participation that provides political cover without institutional depth.



Ireland risks the same outcome if layered cooperation remains at the governance level without generating operational capability. Seven PESCO projects on paper means nothing if those projects do not produce deployed capability that feeds into Ireland’s maritime, subsea, and cyber domains. The Cork MoU is a framework, not a capability. The September 2026 exercises are a beginning, not a system reform. The Austrian lesson: being at the table is not the same as building something at the table.



Figure 4: Ireland and Austria — structurally parallel, strategically divergent. Austria joined PESCO defensively, choosing undemanding projects. Ireland has a chance to do it differently: choosing domain-specific projects that build real capability in its geographic lanes.



The September 2026 exercises as design problem



The joint exercises committed at Cork for September 2026 are the first opportunity to test whether the layered model generates operational capability or remains a governance exercise. What is tested matters as much as whether the exercises succeed. The exercises should test five things.



1. Classification transfer protocols



Can maritime surveillance data generated from joint UK–Ireland patrols be processed and shared into CISE within operational timelines? This requires testing the declassification and sanitisation pathways for UK-origin data entering EU systems. If it takes weeks to clear data for CISE ingestion, the data is operationally useless.



2. Dual-governance incident response



When a subsea cable incident occurs in waters covered by both the bilateral Subsea Framework and the PESCO Critical Seabed Infrastructure Protection project, which governance activates first? The exercise should simulate a cable incident and test whether the response protocols can operate under dual governance without conflicting instructions.



3. Cyber dual-track reporting



Can Ireland’s NCSC simultaneously share detailed threat indicators bilaterally with UK NCSC and processed analysis with PESCO Cyber Threats partners? This tests the institutional capacity for managing classified information across two channels with different classification regimes.



4. Air domain asymmetry



How does air domain information sharing work when one partner has no independent detection capability? The exercise should test the information flow from UK radar coverage into Irish operational awareness — and identify what changes when Irish primary radar becomes operational in 2028.



5. Cross-border coordination



Can island-level agencies in both jurisdictions coordinate a response to a simultaneous maritime and energy infrastructure incident without ad hoc improvisation? This tests whether standing coordination mechanisms exist at the implementation layer — or whether every incident requires governance-level intervention to coordinate the response.

Conditions for replicability



The layered cooperation model is replicable under five conditions. First, geographic domain relevance: the state must occupy a geographic or institutional position that creates a specific security domain where its contribution is irreplaceable. Ireland has the Atlantic EEZ and the cable terminus. Austria has the Alpine transit corridor and the Balkan proximity. The domain must be specific enough to justify domain-focused cooperation rather than full-spectrum participation.



Second, an existing bilateral partner: the state must have a bilateral security relationship with a partner whose capabilities complement its own, and whose cooperation does not undermine EU commitments. Ireland has the UK. Austria has Germany. The bilateral partner need not be an EU member — the UK and Switzerland precedents in PESCO demonstrate this.



Third, EU framework access: the state must participate in PESCO and have access to EU funding instruments (EDF, EDIP, SAFE). This provides the multilateral legitimacy that bilateral cooperation alone cannot generate. Without the EU track, bilateral cooperation risks being seen as dependency rather than partnership.



Fourth, NATO-adjacent engagement: some form of engagement with NATO structures — through PfP, ITPP, or project-specific participation — provides interoperability standards and multilateral reach that EU frameworks alone do not cover.



Fifth, political capacity for the constitutional distinction: the state’s political system must be capable of distinguishing between non-alignment (a diplomatic posture governing alliance membership) and security cooperation (an operational necessity governing domain protection). If the political system conflates these — treating any cooperation as a step toward alliance membership — the model cannot operate. Ireland’s McEntee formulation (‘not neutral to threats’) demonstrates this capacity. Austria’s defensive PESCO posture suggests it may not.



The real risk



Figure 6: Neither track generates sufficient depth alone. EU participation without bilateral depth is symbolic. Bilateral cooperation without EU legitimacy is ad hoc. The layered model works only if both tracks generate institutional depth — and are connected through a functioning implementation layer.
