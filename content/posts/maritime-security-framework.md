## Introduction

Ireland's maritime domain has become increasingly central to national security planning. With over 900 kilometres of coastline and an exclusive economic zone (EEZ) extending to 200 nautical miles encompassing approximately 900,000 square kilometres, Ireland commands one of Europe's largest maritime territories. This vast maritime domain represents substantial economic opportunity—fisheries, renewable energy development, critical infrastructure corridors—but also emerging security challenges that require systematic strategic reassessment.

The security environment surrounding Irish maritime interests has undergone profound transformation in recent years. Russian naval activity in European waters has intensified, creating new strategic complexity. Maritime commerce disruption patterns have evolved with geopolitical tensions affecting chokepoints and shipping routes. Climate-driven changes in ocean conditions are shifting fish stocks and creating new patterns of maritime activity. Simultaneously, critical infrastructure—from submarine cables carrying global internet and financial traffic to undersea pipelines—has become increasingly concentrated in maritime zones where Ireland bears responsibility for monitoring and protection.

Ireland's historical approach to maritime security, shaped by Cold War-era neutrality and post-Cold War assumptions of a benign strategic environment, is increasingly misaligned with contemporary realities. This paper examines the current maritime security framework, identifies capability gaps and strategic vulnerabilities, and provides evidence-based recommendations for updating Ireland's maritime security posture to reflect 21st-century strategic and operational realities.

## The Strategic Context

Ireland's maritime position occupies a unique position in European strategic geography. To the west lies the Atlantic, with direct access to North American sea lanes and significant distance from major power competition. To the east, the Irish Sea and Celtic Sea connect to the English Channel, North Sea, and beyond—the busiest shipping lanes in Europe. To the north, the waters around Northern Ireland and Scotland interface with NATO maritime operations and potential Russian naval activity.

This strategic position creates simultaneous opportunities and vulnerabilities. Economically, the maritime zones generate value through fisheries (approximately 12,000 jobs), aquaculture, and emerging renewable energy (wind, wave, tidal). These economic assets require protection. Geopolitically, the maritime domain places Ireland at the interface between Atlantic and European strategic systems—a position of influence and responsibility.

### Emerging Maritime Threats

**Russian Naval Activity**: Russian naval presence in European waters has increased substantially since 2022, with expanded submarine operations, surface ship deployments, and underwater reconnaissance activity. Some Russian naval operations have extended into the North Atlantic, creating potential proximity to Irish maritime zones. While direct Russian military threat to Ireland remains low, the presence of Russian naval capability in adjacent waters increases baseline strategic complexity.

**Critical Infrastructure Vulnerability**: Submarine cables carrying digital traffic (internet, financial systems, data services) and undersea pipelines carrying energy resources have become critical strategic infrastructure. These systems are concentrated in European maritime zones and are potentially vulnerable to disruption—either through state actors with undersea capability (Russia possesses advanced underwater systems) or through non-state actors with growing capability in underwater technology.

**Illicit Maritime Activity**: Illegal fishing in Irish waters has increased, particularly from vessels operating outside regulatory frameworks. The COVID-19 pandemic exposed the scale of this vulnerability when significant fishing activity occurred with minimal Irish capacity to respond. Beyond fishing, illicit transport of goods and people through maritime zones represents emerging security challenge.

**Hybrid Threats**: Maritime domain represents a potential vector for hybrid threats—disinformation campaigns related to maritime issues, cyberattacks on maritime infrastructure, economic pressure through shipping disruption. The 2022 disruption of Baltic maritime commerce due to sanctions on Russian shipping provides illustrative example.

**Environmental and Climate Change**: Changing ocean conditions are affecting fish stocks and maritime patterns, creating new boundaries of uncertainty in maritime security planning. Climate-driven changes to sea ice, currents, and seasonal patterns will continue reshaping maritime strategic geography.

## Current Maritime Security Framework

Ireland's maritime security responsibilities are distributed across multiple institutions, creating a fragmented framework that, while functional for traditional peacetime operations, shows strain under contemporary pressures.

### Institutional Structure

**The Irish Defence Forces Naval Service** operates the primary military maritime presence. The Naval Service comprises approximately 1,100 personnel (about 12 per cent of defence forces total strength) and maintains an aging fleet: the Oileán Gheálach-class offshore patrol vessels (3 vessels, commissioned 1997-2000) and Eithne-class vessels for coastal patrol (2 vessels, commissioned 2002-2003). These vessels provide visible maritime presence and can conduct fisheries protection and basic law enforcement operations, but are insufficient for continuous surveillance of the 900,000 square kilometre EEZ.

**The Irish Coast Guard**, operating under the Department of Transport, has responsibility for search and rescue, maritime law enforcement, and civil maritime safety. The Coast Guard operates smaller patrol vessels and maritime helicopters, coordinating with the Naval Service on maritime safety and law enforcement operations.

**Supporting Assets** include maritime patrol aircraft (limited availability), periodic access to NATO maritime surveillance through EU-NATO cooperation, and informal intelligence sharing with international partners. However, systematic, continuous maritime surveillance capability remains limited.

### Capability Gaps

The current maritime security framework faces several critical capability gaps:

**Surveillance**: Ireland cannot conduct continuous, systematic surveillance of its entire EEZ. The Naval Service vessels can conduct periodic patrols, but these provide presence rather than persistent awareness. A vessel conducting surface patrols can cover perhaps 5,000-10,000 square kilometres per day—equivalent to roughly 0.5-1 per cent of the total EEZ. Continuous coverage would require either a fleet of dozens of vessels (cost-prohibitive) or advanced technology systems (currently absent).

**Underwater Detection**: Ireland has no dedicated submarine detection capability and no anti-submarine warfare systems. Detection of submarine activity relies on occasional NATO submarine movements detected through informal intelligence channels, but systematic monitoring of Irish submarine zones is absent.

**Air Defence Integration**: The Naval Service has no integral air defence, making the fleet vulnerable to air attack and dependent on external air defence support (limited to NATO cooperative arrangements). Modern naval operations assume integrated air and maritime defence; Ireland's naval vessels operate without this integration.

**Information Integration**: Surveillance systems, coast guard operations, fishing vessel position data (AIS—Automatic Identification System), and maritime traffic information are not integrated into unified maritime domain awareness. Multiple agencies operate systems, but cross-agency information sharing and integrated operational picture are limited.

**Personnel and Training**: The Naval Service operates below required personnel strength (approximately 1,100 vs. 1,300-1,400 required for sustainable operations). Personnel retention is challenging in maritime specialisations, creating expertise gaps.

## Strategic Vulnerabilities

The gaps in Ireland's maritime security framework create several strategic vulnerabilities:

**Economic Vulnerability**: Fisheries worth billions of euro annually and emerging renewable energy sectors operate in maritime zones where Ireland cannot adequately protect against illicit activity or ensure sustainable resource management. Illegal fishing continues with minimal consequence, undermining both economic value and conservation objectives.

**Infrastructure Vulnerability**: Critical submarine cables and pipelines pass through Irish maritime zones with limited security oversight. While wholesale cable damage remains unlikely, the possibility of disruption—through state actors, environmental damage, or commercial accidents—creates vulnerability for systems Ireland depends upon but cannot fully protect.

**Sovereignty Assertion**: The absence of credible Irish maritime surveillance means that Irish sovereignty in maritime zones is asserted primarily through claims and international law rather than through demonstrated capability. Other states can conduct activities in Irish maritime zones with limited immediate detection or response capability.

**NATO Integration Challenges**: NATO maritime operations in North Atlantic and approaches to Europe assume contributions from all members. Ireland's limited maritime capability creates challenges for alliance interoperability and collective defence assumptions.

## Technical and Operational Solutions

Addressing maritime security gaps requires investment in both technology and operational capacity. Several proven approaches offer potential solutions:

### Technology-Based Options

**Autonomous Surface Vehicles (ASVs)**: Uncrewed surface vessels can conduct extended surveillance missions at fraction of cost of crewed vessels. Several European countries have begun pilot programmes with promising results. ASVs can carry surveillance payloads (radar, optical sensors, acoustic equipment) and can operate for weeks or months, providing persistent coverage far exceeding crewed vessel capability. Costs are modest compared to traditional patrol vessels—€5-15 million for equipped ASVs vs. €50-150 million for crewed patrol vessels.

**Satellite-Based Monitoring**: European satellite programs (Copernicus program operated by European Commission, and emerging commercial satellite providers) now provide substantial maritime monitoring capability. Satellite radar can detect vessel movements and some subsurface activity; optical satellites can identify vessel types and activities. Ireland's utilisation of these systems is currently limited but could be substantially expanded. Integration of satellite data with other maritime information would significantly improve awareness.

**Integrated Maritime Domain Awareness**: Comprehensive systems combining vessel automatic identification systems (AIS), naval radar, fishing vessel position reporting, environmental monitoring, and cooperative information sharing with neighbouring states can provide real-time situational awareness across the EEZ. Such systems operate in several European countries (Netherlands, Denmark, Portugal) and have proven effective at relatively modest cost (€20-50 million initial investment, €3-5 million annual operation).

**Underwater Detection Systems**: Fixed underwater acoustic arrays or towed array sonar systems can detect submarine activity. These systems range from relatively modest autonomous underwater vehicles with acoustic payloads (€5-10 million) to larger integrated networks (€50-100 million). Smaller deployments could provide detection capability in strategically important areas.

### Operational Solutions

**Expanded Maritime Patrol**: The Naval Service could expand its maritime patrol capacity through modest fleet expansion or through partnerships with other European states sharing maritime patrol assets. Nordic maritime cooperation models demonstrate how smaller states share patrol assets cost-effectively.

**Coast Guard Expansion**: The Irish Coast Guard could take on expanded maritime security responsibilities, with proper training and equipment. Several European countries integrate coast guard and naval maritime operations more fully than Ireland does.

**European Partnership and PESCO**: Ireland participates in European maritime security initiatives and PESCO maritime projects. Deepening these partnerships creates access to maritime surveillance and operations capacity beyond Irish national capability.

**Port Security and Supply Chain Monitoring**: Enhanced monitoring of maritime supply chains and port activities can detect illicit activity and protect critical infrastructure.

## Policy Recommendations

Based on this analysis, Ireland should pursue a comprehensive maritime security strategy incorporating three elements:

### 1. Maintain Core Naval Presence

The Naval Service should maintain and upgrade its operational fleet, modernising patrol vessels as they age and ensuring sufficient personnel for sustained operations. The Oileán Gheálach-class vessels, commissioned in the late 1990s, require replacement or major upgrade within the next 5-10 years. Replacement vessels should include enhanced capability for integrated maritime operations and improved integration with other security forces.

### 2. Develop Technology-Based Surveillance Capability

Ireland should invest in technology systems that provide persistent maritime awareness without requiring massive vessel flotillas. Priority should be given to:

- Integrated maritime domain awareness system consolidating multiple data sources (AIS, radar, satellite, environmental data)
- Autonomous surface vehicles for extended surveillance missions in strategic areas
- Enhanced utilisation of European satellite monitoring systems
- Acoustic monitoring systems for underwater detection in key strategic areas

Total investment for these systems would be €50-100 million over 5 years, with annual operating costs of €8-12 million. This represents approximately 5-10 per cent of the naval operations budget but would provide significantly enhanced capability.

### 3. Deepen European Partnership and Integration

Ireland should:

- Expand participation in PESCO maritime security initiatives
- Deepen cooperation with European partners on maritime domain awareness
- Participate more actively in NATO maritime operations through EU-NATO cooperation mechanisms
- Develop bilateral maritime security partnerships with key partners (France, Spain, Portugal, UK, Nordic states)

These partnerships would provide access to maritime capability exceeding Irish national resources and would integrate Irish maritime security into broader European maritime security framework.

## Implications for Defence Investment and Strategic Autonomy

The maritime security challenges outlined in this paper require sustained investment in maritime capability. This investment serves multiple strategic objectives: protection of Irish economic interests in maritime zones, contribution to European maritime security and strategic autonomy, integration into NATO collective defence frameworks, and assertion of Irish sovereignty and jurisdiction in national maritime territory.

The investment required is substantial but manageable within the defence budget framework outlined in the Levels of Ambition. Maritime capability development is explicitly included in LOA 2 (monitoring capability) and LOA 3 (active maritime defence). The recommendations in this paper provide a roadmap for converting the abstract capability targets in the LOA framework into concrete systems and operational improvements.

## Conclusion

Ireland's maritime security framework must evolve to meet contemporary strategic and operational realities. The current framework, adequate for traditional peacetime operations and fisheries protection, is increasingly strained by emerging maritime challenges—critical infrastructure protection, submarine presence, hybrid threats, and illicit maritime activity.

The evolution required is not revolutionary. Other European states of similar size and resources have developed comprehensive maritime security capability. The investment required is substantial but manageable within contemporary defence budget frameworks. The technology solutions are proven—other European countries have demonstrated their effectiveness. The institutional mechanisms for European partnership are established.

What is required is strategic choice: a decision that maritime security matters, that Irish maritime domain deserves persistent surveillance and active security management, and that Ireland should develop capability—through national investment and European partnership—to protect its maritime interests. The full paper provides detailed analysis of capability options, institutional mechanisms, and implementation roadmaps for achieving this strategic objective.
