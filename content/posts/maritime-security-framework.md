## Strategic Context: Ireland's Maritime Domain

Ireland commands one of Europe's largest maritime jurisdictions. The exclusive economic zone (EEZ) extends 900,000 square kilometres, stretching westward to the mid-Atlantic, southward toward the Bay of Biscay, and northward toward Arctic shipping lanes. Within this domain, Ireland has sovereign rights to exploit natural resources, regulate commercial activity, and exercise jurisdiction. Beyond the EEZ lies international waters, but Ireland has interests in those waters: fishing rights through international agreements, environmental management responsibilities, and security interests in critical infrastructure (subsea cables, pipelines) and shipping lanes.

The strategic importance of this maritime domain has grown dramatically. Russian aggression in Ukraine has created concerns about Russian naval activity in Atlantic waters. Hybrid threats—disinformation, infrastructure targeting, economic coercion—extend into maritime domain. Critical subsea infrastructure (internet cables, energy pipelines) concentrated in European waters creates vulnerabilities. And the transition to renewable energy has created new maritime infrastructure requiring protection.

Within this changing environment, Ireland's maritime security framework remains historically constrained: two maritime patrol aircraft, three frigates reaching end-of-life, minimal underwater surveillance, and no integrated command-and-control system linking maritime operations with air and cyber domains.

## Capabilities Assessment: Current State

### Operational Assets

**Maritime Patrol Aircraft**: The Defence Force operates two Cessna P-8 Poseidon maritime patrol aircraft equipped with sophisticated surveillance sensors (radar, magnetic anomaly detection, forward-looking infrared, acoustic buoys). These aircraft have global deployability and long endurance (6+ hours on station). However, two aircraft cannot provide continuous coverage. With maintenance downtime and crew training requirements, operational availability is typically 50–60%, meaning one aircraft is operational on any given day. Coverage of a 900,000 km² EEZ requires approximately 24–30 hours of weekly patrol coverage. Two aircraft, when fully available, can provide approximately 12 hours weekly.

**Surface Vessels**: The Defence Force operates a frigate squadron of three ships (Carraighe, Orla, Eithne) designed for general-purpose operations. These vessels have age profiles ranging from 15 to 40+ years, with limited sensor suites and outdated weapons systems. While suitable for presence operations and goodwill visits, they have limited capability for sustained surveillance or tactical response to modern threats. Additionally, frigate deployments typically follow rotation patterns (one deployed, one in work-up, one in maintenance), meaning only one vessel is operational at any given time. Maintenance challenges have recently extended downtime for all three vessels.

**Coastal radar and surveillance**: Ireland operates coastal radar systems distributed along the coast, primarily for shipping traffic management rather than military surveillance. These systems can detect large surface vessels but have limited capability against small boats, submarines, or maritime drones. Coverage is incomplete in remote coastal areas.

### Operational Constraints

The combined assets create significant gaps:

- **Temporal coverage**: Two patrol aircraft and one frigate cannot provide continuous surveillance. Gaps exist where no surveillance is occurring.
- **Depth**: Surveillance extends to surface and air; underwater monitoring is absent. Submarines and underwater drones are undetected.
- **Integration**: Maritime assets operate independently without integrated command-and-control linking air, surface, and cyber elements into unified operational picture.
- **Response capacity**: Detection capability is limited without commensurate response capacity. The Defence Force can detect a suspicious vessel; responding meaningfully requires assets (boarding teams, patrol boats) not currently available.

## Institutional Actors and Coordination

Maritime security in Ireland involves multiple institutions:

**Defence Forces**: Primary responsibility for military maritime security, operating patrol aircraft, frigates, and coastal radar.

**Naval Service**: Dedicated maritime operations command within the Defence Forces, managing frigate operations and coastal surveillance.

**Irish Coast Guard**: Civilian maritime search-and-rescue and environmental response, with vessels and helicopters capable of rapid response.

**Fisheries Protection Service**: Monitoring of fishing activities and enforcement of maritime regulations, operating patrol vessels.

**Customs and Excise**: Border security in maritime domain, intercepting smuggling and contraband.

**An Garda Síochána**: Law enforcement authority with maritime police units for coastal crimes.

**Department of Transport**: Administration of maritime traffic management, maritime infrastructure, and shipping regulation.

These institutions operate under different command structures, funding streams, and operational frameworks. Coordination mechanisms exist (maritime security committees, interagency task forces) but are not institutionalised at strategic level. This fragmentation reduces efficiency and creates gaps.

## Strategic Challenges: Severity and Evolution

### Challenge 1: Russian Naval Activity

Russia operates significant naval assets in Atlantic waters including submarines, surface vessels, and reconnaissance aircraft. Russian vessels transit through Irish EEZ occasionally; Russian submarines operate in Atlantic depths with increasing frequency. While Russian naval activity is not directed specifically at Ireland, it creates awareness of undersea activity and potential vulnerability to Russian intelligence operations targeting Irish infrastructure or strategic communication.

### Challenge 2: Hybrid Threats and Critical Infrastructure

Subsea cables connecting Ireland to Europe and North America carry internet traffic and financial transactions. Pipeline infrastructure transports energy. These critical systems are vulnerable to deliberate damage or targeting in conflicts. Russian hybrid warfare operations against other European states have included infrastructure sabotage (though not confirmed against Irish infrastructure to date). The threat is credible.

### Challenge 3: Non-state Actors and Trafficking

Organised crime networks use maritime domain for drug trafficking, human smuggling, and contraband movement. These operations are not military threats but create security concerns and law enforcement demand. Enhanced maritime awareness would improve law enforcement effectiveness.

### Challenge 4: Environmental and Fisheries Security

Climate change is shifting fish stocks northward, creating increased economic activity and geopolitical competition in northern waters. Illegal fishing in Irish waters costs millions annually. Maritime surveillance enhances enforcement capability.

## Strategic Recommendations: Three Elements

### Element 1: Equipment Modernisation

The Defence Force requires:

- **Additional maritime patrol aircraft**: One additional P-8 Poseidon or equivalent would increase coverage to approximately 18 hours weekly, meaningful improvement from current 12 hours.
- **Underwater surveillance capability**: Autonomous underwater vehicles (AUVs) or buoy-based passive sonar systems would enable subsea monitoring, currently absent.
- **Modern frigate class**: Three new multipurpose frigates to replace aging Carraighe-class vessels would provide modern sensors, weapons systems, and sustained surveillance capability.
- **Coastal patrol boats**: Rapid-response patrol boats would enable response to detected threats and law enforcement support.
- **Integrated command-and-control system**: Unified system linking all maritime sensors, aircraft, vessels, and cyber elements into single operational picture managed from central command centre.

Capital cost is substantial: modern frigate approximately €500–600 million each (€1.5–1.8 billion for three-ship class); additional patrol aircraft approximately €200–300 million; AUVs and buoy systems approximately €50–100 million; patrol boats approximately €50–100 million total; command-and-control system approximately €100–150 million. Total would be approximately €2.5–3 billion over 10 years—substantial but achievable from existing capital plans with prioritisation.

### Element 2: Personnel and Specialisation

Maritime security requires specialised personnel: naval officers, pilots, sensor operators, maintenance engineers, cyber specialists. Current Defence Force strength of 7,300 is insufficient to staff all maritime elements while maintaining other capabilities. Expansion to 8,500 (LOA 2 target) and potentially 10,000+ (LOA 3 target) would create capacity for maritime specialisation. Additionally, specialised training programmes in maritime warfare, undersea operations, and integrated command-and-control are required.

### Element 3: Institutional Coordination and Framework

Maritime security should be institutionalised through:

- **Maritime Security Strategy**: Formal government document defining maritime security objectives, institutional roles, resource allocation, and international coordination
- **Maritime Operations Centre**: Central command facility coordinating all maritime security operations, integrating Defence Forces, Coast Guard, Fisheries Protection, and Customs operations
- **Interagency Maritime Task Force**: Regular coordination mechanism at strategic level, chaired by Department of Defence or Department of Enterprise
- **EU and international cooperation**: Structured engagement with EU maritime security initiatives (PESCO maritime operations, EU Military Maritime Operations Headquarters participation), NATO coordination, and bilateral cooperation with UK and other partners

## European Context: Comparison and Positioning

Ireland's maritime security posture is weak compared to comparable European states:

- **UK**: Royal Navy operates 2 aircraft carriers, 6 modern Type 45 destroyers, 13 modern frigates, and extensive submarine fleet. Maritime patrol capability includes aircraft, helicopters, and fixed-wing reconnaissance. UK maritime budget exceeds €5 billion annually.
- **France**: French Navy operates 1 aircraft carrier, multiple destroyers and frigates, and extensive submarine fleet. Maritime patrol includes aircraft and helicopters. France has significant marine engineering industrial base. French maritime budget exceeds €3.5 billion annually.
- **Germany**: German Navy operates 6 modern frigates, multiple submarines, and maritime patrol aircraft. German maritime budget exceeds €1.5 billion annually.
- **Denmark**: Danish Navy operates 6 frigates, submarine fleet, and maritime patrol aircraft. Denmark has maritime industrial presence. Danish maritime budget exceeds €500 million annually.

Ireland operates 3 aging frigates, 2 patrol aircraft, and limited coastal surveillance. Irish maritime budget is approximately €200–250 million annually (portion of total defence budget). The disparity reflects Ireland's historical non-alignment and limited defence ambition. However, as threats have evolved, the gap has become strategically significant.

## Timeline and Implementation

Maritime security modernisation should follow phased approach:

**2026–2028 (Phase 1)**: Acquisition of additional maritime patrol aircraft (one additional P-8 Poseidon or equivalent), beginning underwater surveillance system deployment, establishment of Maritime Operations Centre, interagency coordination framework institutionalisation.

**2028–2030 (Phase 2)**: First new frigate delivery, expansion of underwater surveillance to full coverage, command-and-control system integration, personnel expansion to support expanded capabilities.

**2030–2035 (Phase 3)**: Delivery of remaining frigates, full operational integration, expansion to LOA 3 level maritime security capability.

This timeline assumes sustained political commitment and adequate capital funding. Any significant change in political priorities or budget constraints would extend timelines.

## Conclusion: Maritime Security as Strategic Priority

Ireland's maritime domain is a strategic asset: 900,000 km² of EEZ with natural resource value, shipping lane importance, and critical infrastructure requiring protection. Current maritime security capabilities are inadequate for this domain and inconsistent with Ireland's position as an EU member state and developed economy.

Modernisation is required on three dimensions: equipment (patrol aircraft, frigates, underwater surveillance), personnel (maritime specialists, expanded Defence Force), and institutional (coordination mechanisms, formal strategy, command-and-control integration). The commitment is substantial but achievable within existing fiscal frameworks.

The strategic logic is clear: Ireland faces maritime security challenges (Russian activity, infrastructure vulnerability, law enforcement needs) requiring enhanced capability. The operational logic is sound: additional aircraft, modern frigates, and integrated systems would meaningfully improve coverage and response capacity. The financial logic is feasible: maritime modernisation can be integrated into existing capital plans with appropriate prioritisation.

Whether the political will exists to sustain this commitment remains the critical question. The answer will determine whether Ireland's maritime domain remains under-secured or becomes a strategic asset fully developed and protected.
