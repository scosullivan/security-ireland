## Where the Money Comes From, Where It Goes, and Why the Constraint Isn't Budget

Ireland's defence and security funding flows through five distinct channels. Understanding which channel funds what explains why the system is configured the way it is — and where the leverage points sit.

![Ireland's defence spending compared to EU and NATO peers, 2025](/images/charts/ids_gdp_comparison.png)

### 1. The National Defence Budget

This is the core. Ireland's defence vote covers personnel, operations, and capital. The capital programme — €1.7 billion over 2026–2030 — is the transformative element. Historically, the capital budget was negligible: equipment replacement, barracks maintenance, minor procurement. The Level of Ambition 2 commitment has changed this into a genuine capability-building programme.

Even at the enhanced level, Ireland's total defence investment is a rounding error by European standards. Germany's debt brake reform alone unlocked €400 billion. The SAFE instrument offers €150 billion. Ireland's entire decade-long programme is smaller than what many individual member states spend in a single year.

What the budget funds: personnel costs for 7,531 permanent Defence Forces (against a 9,739 establishment); the primary radar programme (targeting 2028); counter-drone procurement (summer 2026); naval fleet renewal; armoured vehicle procurement; reserve reform; overseas peacekeeping sustainment.

What it cannot fund: the institutional transformation required to absorb the equipment. A procurement system designed for routine replacement is now running first-generation capability builds in domains it has never operated in. No template, no precedent, no institutional memory.

### 2. EU Instruments

PESCO: Ireland participates in 7 projects as a full member: Maritime Surveillance Upgrade, Maritime Mine Countermeasures, Critical Seabed Infrastructure Protection, Cyber Threats and Incident Response, and Network of Logistic Hubs. PESCO is the only EU framework where Ireland's defence contribution is formally recognised.

SAFE (€150bn loans): Ireland is eligible for SAFE loans for maritime and cyber procurement. Preferential terms for joint procurement could reduce costs. Phase 2 terms, negotiated during Ireland's Presidency, determine whether domain-specific procurement qualifies.

EDIP (€1.5bn grants): Implementing rules being written during Ireland's Presidency determine whether 'defence industrial participation' includes technology and dual-use contribution. If so, EDIP becomes accessible. If not, Ireland is excluded.

EDF (€8bn R&D): Collaborative research in cyber, AI, and maritime sensing. Feeds future procurement pipelines.

### 3. Bilateral UK–Ireland Cooperation

The Cork Summit of March 2026 operationalised bilateral security cooperation for the first time. The refreshed MoU covers maritime cooperation, cyber defence, air domain information sharing, and joint procurement; government-to-government sales bypass the commercial procurement process Ireland's system is least equipped to manage. Personnel and training exchanges build institutional capacity that money alone cannot buy.

### 4. NATO-Adjacent Frameworks

Ireland joined NATO's Individually Tailored Partnership Programme in 2024 and applied in March 2026 to the North Sea Joint Declaration on subsea security. These provide access to standards, exercises, and intelligence-sharing. The precedent: Switzerland's neutral but extensively networked model.

### 5. CISE Maritime Data Infrastructure

Ireland joined CISE in April 2025. Access to maritime surveillance data partially compensates for the Naval Service's inability to physically patrol its 880,000 km² EEZ. Infrastructure, not funding — but it makes every euro spent on maritime surveillance more effective.

Source: Author's analysis. Dept of Defence, EU PESCO documentation, Cork Summit communiqué, NATO.

## Where the Money Goes

Ireland's defence and security ambitions span seven domains. Each is at a different stage, faces different constraints, and draws on different funding channels.

### Airspace

Current state: Ireland cannot detect aircraft entering its airspace. No primary radar, no fighters, no surface-to-air missiles. Under a secret arrangement, the RAF intercepts hostile aircraft — sometimes without prior Irish awareness.

Being built: Primary radar programme targeting 2028 — Ireland's first-ever air surveillance. Counter-drone for summer 2026. Funded by national capital budget; potentially SAFE-eligible.

Constraint: First-generation capability build in a procurement system with no comparable experience.

### Maritime

Current state: Nine vessels, no functioning armament, one large vessel deployable. No anti-submarine capability. 880,000 km² of EEZ with less than 15% surveillance coverage on any given day.

Being built: Fleet renewal in the capital programme. PESCO Maritime Surveillance Upgrade and Mine Countermeasures. CISE data layer. Presidency objective: host the Atlantic CISE node.

Constraint: Personnel. Starting salary €40,000 where rent near barracks exceeds €1,800/month. No vessel procurement solves this.

### Cyber & Hybrid Resilience

Ireland hosts the EU headquarters of most major American tech companies. A cyber attack on Irish-hosted systems affects the entire Union. The 2021 HSE ransomware attack cost hundreds of millions. The NCSC remains under-resourced. The government is removing restrictions on Enterprise Ireland's dual-use engagement. If EDIP's rules recognise cyber and AI, Ireland's tech sector becomes a legitimate contributor to EU defence.

### Intelligence Architecture

Split across three agencies with no unified national security structure. Unique in Europe. The forthcoming Intelligence Architecture Reform paper examines what unification would require. This is the deepest structural reform on the agenda — years, not months.

### Personnel

7,531 against a 9,739 establishment, indicating a fifty-year low as recruitment competes with Dublin's tech sector. Every capability domain requires people: radar needs operators, vessels need crews, cyber needs specialists. Without solving personnel, every capital investment underperforms.

### Defence Industrial Base

Ireland has no defence industrial base. But the €1.7 billion capital programme and the IDSA's €2.5 billion estimate create procurement demand for the first time. The question: can the institutional prerequisites be built fast enough to convert this demand into domestic capacity?

## The Three Cooperation Tracks

Ireland's security architecture operates through three tracks that feed into each other:

EU frameworks (PESCO, CISE, EDIP/SAFE eligibility) provide institutional legitimacy and funding access. Bilateral UK–Ireland cooperation (Cork MoU, Subsea Framework, exercises) builds operational capacity.

NATO-adjacent frameworks (ITPP, North Sea Declaration) connect Ireland to alliance standards without membership.

Ireland's Presidency narrative: 'We contribute to European security through structured cooperation in the domains where our geography makes us critical — maritime surveillance, subsea protection, cyber resilience — using bilateral, EU, and NATO-adjacent partnerships that generate real capability without compromising our constitutional position.'

## What's Missing

No National Security Strategy. Ireland remains one of few EU states without one.

No LOA transition roadmap. 54 of 130 Commission recommendations completed in four years. Rate slowing.

No permanent financing architecture. Unlike Germany's constitutional reform, Ireland's programme can be reversed.

The commander-in-chief problem. President Connolly is constitutionally Supreme Commander while opposed to the military's existence.
