## What is PESCO?

The Permanent Structured Cooperation on Defence (PESCO) is a European Union mechanism that allows member states to deepen defence collaboration and military capabilities. Established under the Lisbon Treaty in 2009, PESCO enables groups of member states to work together on military operations, capability development, and defence research without requiring agreement from all 27 EU states.

Think of PESCO as a "coalition builder" within the EU framework. While EU decisions typically require consensus, PESCO allows smaller groups of willing states to pursue deeper integration on defence matters. Currently, 26 of the EU's 27 member states participate in PESCO; only Denmark has opted out.

## How Does It Work?

PESCO operates through a series of projects and initiatives that participating states voluntarily join. These projects span capabilities development, training and exercises, military mobility, cyber defence, and maritime security. States commit to specific objectives within projects and contribute resources—military personnel, equipment, expertise—according to agreed frameworks.

The key innovation is "structured cooperation" rather than ad-hoc coalitions. This means commitments are formalized, measured, and subject to regular evaluation. It also means that smaller states like Ireland can participate alongside larger European powers in developing defence capabilities and operational capacity.

## Why PESCO Matters for Ireland

For Ireland, PESCO participation represents a carefully calibrated step toward deeper European engagement on defence. It allows Ireland to:

- **Develop new defence capabilities** through collaborative projects without requiring massive independent investment
- **Deepen European security relationships** without formal military alliance commitments
- **Contribute to European security** in a structured, sustainable manner
- **Access training and expertise** from other EU members
- **Participate in capacity building** in neighbouring EU states

Importantly, PESCO participation does not compromise Irish military neutrality as traditionally understood. It represents a form of structured defence cooperation grounded in EU institutions and democratic oversight.

## Current Irish Participation

Ireland participates in numerous PESCO projects covering maritime security, cyber operations, and military mobility. These projects represent Ireland's most significant defence cooperation commitments since the Good Friday Agreement. They signal a strategic choice: that Ireland's security interests are best served through coordinated, multilateral approaches rooted in European democratic institutions.

As Ireland holds the EU Presidency in 2026, PESCO cooperation takes on additional significance. Ireland can shape the trajectory of European defence cooperation, advocating for approaches that prioritize civilian control, democratic accountability, and solutions to genuine security challenges.

## Key Takeaway

PESCO is a framework for voluntary, structured defence cooperation among EU member states. For Ireland, it represents a pragmatic, multilateral approach to security that respects democratic principles while enabling meaningful participation in European security efforts.
