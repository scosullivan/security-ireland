## The Foundation of Modern Irish Security

The Good Friday Agreement of 1998 represented a watershed moment in Irish security policy. By ending over two decades of conflict on the island, it created the conditions for Ireland to reimagine its role in European security. What was once a framework designed to manage internal conflict has become the foundation upon which modern Irish security architecture rests.

Yet the security environment of 2026 bears little resemblance to that of 1998. The emergence of hybrid threats, the digital revolution, and Ireland's deepening integration into European security structures have all challenged the assumptions embedded in the GFA's security provisions. This paper examines how policymakers and security practitioners are adapting the Good Friday Agreement framework to address contemporary challenges while maintaining its core commitment to non-violence and democratic accountability.

## Adapting to Hybrid Threats

The first major challenge to the post-GFA security consensus has come from the rise of hybrid threats. Unlike the terrorism of the 1990s, hybrid warfare encompasses cyber attacks, disinformation campaigns, economic coercion, and the exploitation of internal divisions. These threats do not neatly fit the categories of analysis that drove security policy in 1998.

The COVID-19 pandemic exposed significant vulnerabilities in Ireland's ability to respond to complex, cross-sectoral threats. When critical infrastructure—from healthcare to energy—faced simultaneous pressure, it became clear that the traditional security apparatus needed fundamental restructuring. The creation of the National Security Council and the development of comprehensive national security strategies represented an acknowledgment that security could no longer be conceived purely in military or law enforcement terms.

## European Integration and Strategic Alignment

Perhaps the most profound shift in Irish security policy since the Good Friday Agreement has been the acceleration of European defence cooperation. Ireland's participation in the Permanent Structured Cooperation (PESCO) on defence, its increasing alignment with EU security initiatives, and its developing relationship with NATO through the EU represent a significant evolution from the military neutrality that defined Irish foreign policy throughout the Cold War.

This integration with European security structures does not contradict the spirit of the Good Friday Agreement. Rather, it reflects a pragmatic recognition that Irish security cannot be divorced from European security. In an era of Russian aggression, hybrid threats, and potential great power competition, Ireland's interests are best served through coordinated, multilateral approaches rooted in European democratic institutions.

## Democratic Accountability and Civil Control

At the heart of the Good Friday Agreement was a commitment to democratic governance and civilian control of security forces. These principles remain as important today as they were in 1998. As Ireland expands its security capabilities and deepens its participation in international security structures, maintaining robust civilian oversight and parliamentary scrutiny is essential.

The debate over Ireland's participation in PESCO and potential NATO engagement has highlighted the importance of continuing a wide public conversation about security policy. Democratic societies are strongest when security decisions are made transparently, with full public engagement and parliamentary scrutiny. Ireland's tradition of robust, informed security debate—visible in Dáil debates and public discourse—represents a significant strength that must be carefully preserved as policy evolves.

## Conclusion

The Good Friday Agreement established a framework for peace based on democratic principles, respect for diversity, and non-violence. Twenty-eight years later, these principles remain valid guides for Irish security policy. Yet the operational expression of these principles must evolve to meet contemporary challenges. By adapting security architecture to address hybrid threats, by integrating more fully with European security structures, and by maintaining unwavering commitment to democratic accountability, Ireland can honour the legacy of the Good Friday Agreement while building security for the 21st century.
