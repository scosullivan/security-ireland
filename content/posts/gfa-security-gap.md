## The Cork Bypass

On 13 March 2026, the second UK–Ireland Leaders' Summit at Fota House in Cork produced a refreshed Defence MoU, a new Subsea Infrastructure Bilateral Collaboration Framework, commitments to live exercises, and Ireland's application to the North Sea Joint Declaration. This was the most significant operationalisation of UK–Ireland security cooperation in the post-Brexit period.

![The Cork Bypass](/images/charts/gfa_cork_bypass.png)

Yet Northern Ireland was not an institutional actor in any of it. The MoU was signed by national-level defence ministers. Hilary Benn attended as Secretary of State for Northern Ireland — in his capacity as a UK cabinet minister, not as a representative of NI institutions. There was no Strand Two involvement, no NSMC reference, no cross-border body engagement. The governments chose bilateral instruments when they needed to operationalise security cooperation. They did not consider GFA institutions. The question is why.

## The 1998 Firewall: Why Security Was Excluded

The exclusion of security from the GFA's cross-border cooperation structures was not an oversight. It was a deliberate architectural choice made for compelling reasons in 1998.

![The 1998 Security Firewall](/images/charts/gfa_firewall.png)

When the GFA was negotiated, "security" in the Northern Ireland context meant the conflict itself: paramilitary violence, community-level sectarian threat, decommissioning of weapons, and the political management of organisations that had used force. Security was the most politically charged domain in the entire agreement — the domain where trust was lowest and sovereignty sensitivities highest.

Strand Two was given domains where cross-border cooperation was functional, technical, and politically achievable: inland waterways, food safety, trade, language bodies, EU programmes. These domains were chosen precisely because they sat below the sovereignty threshold. The firewall between Strand Two and security was what made Strand Two possible.

The firewall was inherited, not invented. The formative moment was the end of the Irish Civil War in 1923, when the Free State government effectively disbanded the National Army. That century-old institutional pattern shaped what was imaginable in 1998. Cross-border security cooperation was not just politically difficult; it was outside the institutional vocabulary of both the Irish state and the emerging Northern Ireland settlement.

## What Has Changed: External Threats and the Firewall's Blind Spot

The security threats the GFA was designed around were internal. The security threats that now face the shared domain are external: Russian hybrid activity, subsea infrastructure sabotage, cyber attacks on critical systems, energy system disruption, and hostile state reconnaissance of maritime approaches. These are categorically different from the threats the 1998 firewall was designed to exclude.

Monitoring subsea cables is not cross-border policing. Energy infrastructure security is not decommissioning. Maritime domain awareness in the Irish Sea is not the management of paramilitary organisations. The sovereignty sensitivities that made the 1998 firewall necessary do not attach to the same degree to cooperation against external threats to shared infrastructure.

The Cork MoU commits to coordinated response mechanisms for subsea cable incidents, but the operational consequences play out at the island level. Subsea cables land on coasts in both jurisdictions. The energy grid is coupled across the border through SONI and EirGrid. Maritime domain awareness in the Irish Sea involves assets operating from Cork, Belfast, and Plymouth. The governance has been established at the London–Dublin level, but there is no implementation mechanism for the cross-border dimensions.

## Why Northern Ireland Was Bypassed

The Cork bypass was not a deliberate snub. It was the product of three structural factors. First, defence is not devolved — the MoU involves national-level actors, and Strand Two does not have a natural seat at that table. Second, sovereignty sensitivities remain acute — routing defence cooperation through North/South mechanisms risks triggering concerns about creeping integration on both sides. Third, Strand Two has reliability problems — the Assembly was collapsed from 2017 to 2020 and from 2022 to 2024, and security cooperation requires continuous coordination.

Perhaps most fundamentally, security cooperation simply does not exist in the mental map of what GFA institutions do. The century-old pattern of excluding security from cross-border cooperation has made the exclusion invisible. Northern Ireland was not bypassed because it was considered and found wanting, but because security cooperation through cross-border institutions is outside the institutional imagination of both governments.

## The Prospective Case for Strand Two

Given these constraints, is there a case for Strand Two in the security domain? This paper argues yes, but the case is bounded, specific, and forward-looking.

![Selective Relaxation: Four Infrastructure Gates](/images/charts/gfa_selective_relaxation.png)

**What Strand Two cannot do.** It cannot govern UK–Ireland defence cooperation (a state-to-state function). It cannot be the primary security channel (defence is not devolved). It cannot be the sole vehicle for any function requiring continuous reliability.

**What Strand Two could do.** It could serve as the implementation coordination layer for the island-level operational consequences of commitments made elsewhere. Four specific, bounded functions extend existing Strand Two cooperation:

**Energy infrastructure security** through extension of existing SEM governance. SONI and EirGrid already coordinate grid management across the border. Adding security protocols — threat monitoring, vulnerability assessment, incident coordination — is a technical extension of existing interoperability.

**Transport and infrastructure resilience** through extension of existing cross-border transport cooperation. Ports, roads, and logistics nodes already involve cross-border coordination. Adding vulnerability assessment and shared resilience planning is an administrative extension.

**Maritime environmental monitoring** through dual-use extension of existing environmental cooperation. Environmental sensor networks and data-sharing protocols can be extended to include anomaly detection — unusual vessel activity, unexplained seabed disturbance — providing maritime domain awareness under an environmental rubric.

**Cyber resilience coordination** through standing liaison between UK and Irish NCSCs and NI-based agencies for shared cross-border systems, particularly the energy grid and shared data connectivity.

## The Design Principle: Domain-Specific, Not General

Each function is domain-specific, technically grounded, and rooted in existing Strand Two cooperation. The language matters. "Energy infrastructure resilience coordination" operates in a different political register than "cross-border defence cooperation." "Maritime environmental monitoring with anomaly detection" is a different proposition than "naval intelligence sharing." Strand Two's contribution is not to militarise cross-border cooperation but to ensure that the island-level operational consequences of the Cork MoU have an institutional home.

## The Fallback Architecture

Any proposal that relies on Strand Two must confront its track record of intermittent functionality. The architecture must be designed with fallback mechanisms that function independently of Strand Two's political status.

![The Fallback Architecture](/images/charts/gfa_fallback_architecture.png)

**Primary channel: agency-to-agency protocols.** SONI–EirGrid coordination, NCSC–NCSC liaison, port authority cooperation — these operational relationships function independently of political institutions and are the backbone of the implementation layer.

**Preferred institutional home: Strand Two mechanisms.** When operational, Strand Two provides the formal institutional framework for coordinating agency relationships, hosting review cycles, and managing escalation. Strand Two adds value when it works, but the system must function without it.

**Bilateral backstop: Cork MoU and Subsea Framework.** These function as standing bilateral agreements between sovereign governments. Any implementation function that cannot be reliably carried by Strand Two defaults to the bilateral backstop.

The system degrades gracefully: if Strand Two is offline, operational coordination continues through agency protocols under the bilateral framework. When Strand Two returns, it resumes its coordination function. Security is never hostage to politics.

## Risks

**Unionist concern about creeping integration.** Mitigation: bounded, domain-specific design — energy grid coordination, not defence integration; infrastructure resilience, not military cooperation. The firewall is selectively relaxed, not removed.

**Nationalist concern about cooperation with British military structures.** Mitigation: implementation functions are infrastructure-focused and civilian in character. SONI–EirGrid coordination is not military cooperation.

**Strand Two overload.** Mitigation: incremental extension rather than new mandate — each function builds on existing cooperation rather than creating new institutional structures.

## Conclusion

The 1998 firewall was necessary and it worked. The question now is whether it can be selectively relaxed — lowered in bounded, infrastructure-focused domains where external threats create operational necessity — without compromising the architecture it was designed to protect.

This paper argues that it can, provided the relaxation is domain-specific, technically grounded, and institutionally honest about what Strand Two can and cannot do. The firewall between cross-border cooperation and security was a precondition for the GFA's existence. Selectively lowering it — in bounded domains, for external threats, through infrastructure-focused mechanisms — is now a precondition for the GFA's continued relevance to the security environment in which it must operate.
