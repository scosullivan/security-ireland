## The Cork bypass

On 13 March 2026, the second UK–Ireland Leaders' Summit at Fota House in Cork produced a refreshed Defence and Security MoU. The UK and Irish governments committed to closer liaison between defence and naval organisations, coordinated response to external threats, and information-sharing across all domains.

Yet Northern Ireland was not an institutional actor in any of it.

The MoU was signed by John Healey, UK Defence Secretary, and Helen McEntee, Irish Minister for Defence. The two governments established working groups, committed to quarterly meetings, and tasked officials with developing implementation mechanisms.

The governments chose bilateral and multilateral instruments when they needed to operationalise security cooperation. They did not activate the Good Friday Agreement's cross-border structures.

The question is why.

## The 1998 firewall: why security was excluded

The exclusion of security from the GFA's cross-border cooperation structures was not an oversight. It was a deliberate institutional design choice rooted in the specific political constraints of 1998.

Security meant something different in 1998. When the GFA was negotiated, 'security' in the Northern Irish context meant primarily community-level policing, counterterrorism, and the management of paramilitary activity. Cross-border cooperation on security was politically toxic because it raised questions about jurisdiction, sovereignty, and the role of the British state in Irish territory.

The GFA handled security through entirely separate mechanisms designed for that specific context: the Independent Commission on Policing (which led to the Police Service of Northern Ireland), dedicated counterterrorism protocols, and the explicit exclusion of security matters from Strand Two's cross-border cooperation framework.

Strand Two was designed below the sovereignty threshold. Strand Two was given domains where cross-border cooperation seemed benign: agriculture, education, health, transport, culture. Security was not included because any cross-border security mechanism would have appeared to legitimize either direct Irish state involvement in policing Northern Ireland or, conversely, to accept continued British security control over a territory nationalists hoped would eventually leave the Union.

This was not a failure of ambition but a design principle. The GFA's architecture was intentionally structured to keep security matters out of institutions where they might trigger zero-sum debates about sovereignty.

The firewall was inherited, not invented. The exclusion of security from cross-border institutional frameworks traces back further than 1998. It reflects a century of path dependency: the partition of 1921, the Irish Civil War, and the deliberate institutional separation of the Irish state from British security structures. When the GFA was negotiated, it inherited this separation and made it explicit.

Ireland avoided that fate. But the institutional reasoning became self-reinforcing over a century. The Irish state's founding identity depended on demonstrable separation from British security structures. When the GFA was negotiated, that separation was written into the peace agreement itself.

The GFA's security firewall was therefore the product of two reinforcing histories: the specific political constraints of the 1998 peace process and the longer historical trajectory of Irish state-building through institutional separation from the UK.

## What has changed: external threats and the firewall's blind spot

The security threats the GFA was designed around were internal: paramilitary, community-level, rooted in the legacy of the conflict. They required mechanisms for managing political violence at the community level, strengthening policing capacity, and building cross-community institutions that could survive the transition from conflict.

The security threats that now face the shared domain are external: Russian hybrid activity, subsea infrastructure attacks, cyber warfare, and critical infrastructure interdependencies that cut across the entire island. These threats do not originate from community-level political violence. They originate from strategic competitors probing the vulnerabilities of open Western systems.

These are categorically different from the threats the 1998 firewall was designed to exclude.

Monitoring subsea cables is not cross-border policing; energy infrastructure security is not counterterrorism; cyber resilience coordination is not managing community violence. The firewall was designed to exclude one category of threat. It now creates a blind spot for a different category entirely.

This is important because it defines the scope of any re-instrumentation argument. The claim is not that the GFA's security exclusion was wrong in 1998, but that the threat environment has changed. The mechanisms designed to exclude internal threats now obscure external threats.

The Cork MoU commits to 'closer liaison between defence and naval organisations' and 'coordinated response to external threats.' But it does this through bilateral mechanisms. The governance has been established at the London–Dublin level, but there is no implementation mechanism that connects that governance to the ground-level institutions of Northern Ireland.

This is the implementation gap, which exists because the governance framework used bilateral state-to-state arrangements while the implementation layer would require cross-border coordination. The gap is not political disagreement; it is institutional architecture.

## Why Northern Ireland was bypassed

The Cork bypass was not a deliberate snub, it was the product of three structural factors that this analysis outlines.

**Defence is not devolved.** Strand Two connects Northern Ireland and Ireland, whereas defence cooperation is a state-to-state matter between the UK and Ireland. Northern Ireland's Department of Justice manages policing; it does not manage defence. There is no institutional mechanism in Belfast that can negotiate or implement defence agreements. The relevant UK authority is the Ministry of Defence in London, not any Northern Ireland institution.

**Sovereignty sensitivities remain acute.** Routing defence cooperation through North/South mechanisms raises questions that both governments want to avoid. For unionists, it appears to imply that Irish state authority extends into Northern Ireland's security affairs. For nationalists and the Irish government, it appears to accept British security control as legitimate. The bilateral arrangement sidesteps these disputes.

**Strand Two has reliability problems.** Strand Two mechanisms have been intermittently non-functional due to political disagreement, budget disputes, and the simple fact that institutions requiring consensus between Dublin and Belfast are vulnerable to political breakdown. Any architecture that depends on Strand Two remaining operational is hostage to those politics.

**Security is not in Strand Two's mental map.** Perhaps most fundamentally, security cooperation simply was not considered when the GFA was negotiated and has not been central to Strand Two's development. Strand Two was designed for agriculture, environment, transport, and health. These are the domains where it developed institutional competence. Adding security to Strand Two would require building new expertise and capability in institutions that lack the history to support it.

Northern Ireland was not bypassed because it was considered and found wanting, but because security cooperation was never in the institutional categories that Strand Two was designed to handle.

## The prospective case for Strand Two

Given these constraints, is there any case for Strand Two in the security domain? This paper argues yes, but only for a bounded, specific set of functions.

What Strand Two cannot do. Strand Two cannot govern UK–Ireland defence cooperation, which is a state-level matter. It cannot substitute for bilateral arrangements or NATO coordination. It cannot provide the governance framework that the March 2026 Cork MoU is designed to establish. These functions must remain at the state-to-state level.

What Strand Two could do. Strand Two could serve as the implementation coordination layer for infrastructure resilience and critical security functions that operate at the operational level rather than the governance level. Strand Two already has infrastructure—standing committees, meeting schedules, operational liaison with technical specialists. These structures could be extended to security-adjacent functions where the technical and operational logic already exists.

The specific, bounded functions where Strand Two could add value are those where it already has operational relationships:

**(1) Energy infrastructure security through extension of existing SEM governance.** SONI and EirGrid already coordinate electricity distribution across the island. A standing liaison on grid security, threat monitoring, and incident response could extend existing infrastructure coordination into the security domain without creating new institutional relationships. This is a technical function, not a political one. It builds on existing Strand Two coordination rather than creating new structures.

**(2) Transport and critical infrastructure resilience through extension of existing cross-border transport cooperation.** Strand Two already coordinates on transport, infrastructure resilience, and emergency response. A standing subcommittee on transport and infrastructure security could extend this mandate to cover critical infrastructure interdependencies—port security, border crossing resilience, communications infrastructure. Again, this builds on existing relationships.

**(3) Environmental and maritime monitoring through dual-use extension of existing environmental cooperation.** Strand Two coordinates on environmental matters, including water quality and marine monitoring. Maritime domain awareness and subsea infrastructure monitoring could be framed as environmental coordination with security applications. This leverages existing relationships.

**(4) Cyber resilience coordination through standing liaison between NCSC (UK), NCSC (Ireland), and NI Civil Service IT security.** The two national cyber security centres already have relationships. A Strand Two mechanism could provide the institutional location for coordination on critical infrastructure cyber security, information-sharing, and incident response. This would be technically grounded and would extend existing relationships.

## The design principle: domain-specific, not general

Each of these functions is domain-specific, technically grounded, and rooted in existing Strand Two relationships. None of them involves Strand Two in the governance of UK–Ireland defence cooperation. All of them involve extending existing technical relationships into the security domain.

The language here matters. 'Energy infrastructure resilience coordination' operates in a different category from 'security cooperation.' It is infrastructure coordination with security implications, not security governance. This distinction preserves the firewall's intent—keeping sovereignty disputes out of Strand Two—while creating mechanisms for operational coordination on external threats.

The design principle is: only those functions where Strand Two already has institutional capability and standing relationships, only those where the technical grounding is clear, and only those where the security application is contingent on other functions (energy coordination that happens to improve security) rather than being the primary purpose.

## The fallback architecture

Any proposal that relies on Strand Two must confront its track record of intermittent functionality—political disagreements have caused it to be suspended or severely limited multiple times. The architecture must therefore be designed with fallback mechanisms that function independently of Strand Two's political status.

The architecture must be designed with three layers:

**Primary channel: Agency-to-agency protocols that operate continuously regardless of political context.** Energy operators coordinate with energy operators. Cyber security specialists coordinate with cyber security specialists. These relationships persist even if Strand Two becomes non-functional due to political disagreement. They are technically grounded and do not depend on formal institutional approval to function.

**Preferred institutional home: Strand Two mechanisms, when operational, provide the formal institutional location for these relationships.** When Strand Two is functioning, agency-to-agency protocols can be reported through Strand Two, giving them institutional visibility and political legitimacy. This is the preferred channel because it embeds operational relationships in formal cross-border institutions.

**Bilateral backstop: The Cork MoU and Subsea Infrastructure Framework function as standing bilateral arrangements that can substitute for Strand Two if the institution becomes non-functional.** If Strand Two is suspended due to political disagreement, agency-to-agency protocols and bilateral arrangements continue. The coordination does not stop; it shifts from a cross-border institutional format to a bilateral format.

This design inverts the conventional assumption. The conventional approach treats Strand Two as the governance layer. This approach treats Strand Two as the preferred institutional home for implementation coordination, with bilateral and agency-to-agency relationships as the primary and fallback layers.

## Risks

**Unionist concern about creeping integration.** Security cooperation through North/South mechanisms can be framed as incremental expansion of Irish state authority over Northern Irish security matters. The mitigation is scope clarity: the functions must be genuinely limited to infrastructure resilience and technical coordination, not general security governance.

**Nationalist concern about cooperation with British military structures.** Using GFA institutions to cooperate with British defence organizations may appear to legitimize British security control. The mitigation is institutional framing: the coordination is on technical, infrastructure-focused matters, not on the political question of security authority.

**Strand Two overload.** Adding security-adjacent functions to institutions already carrying significant responsibility for health, education, agriculture, and transport risks degrading all of them. The mitigation is realistic resource allocation: security-adjacent functions should be staffed separately and should not compete for existing Strand Two resources.

**The invisibility risk.** The paper's own analysis shows that security is invisible in the GFA's institutional structures. Routing security-adjacent functions through Strand Two risks making them invisible again—infrastructure resilience committees operating without political attention or accountability. The mitigation is explicit design: security-adjacent functions should be named, reported, and given institutional visibility even if they are routed through Strand Two.

## Conclusion

The GFA's exclusion of security from its cross-border cooperation structures was a deliberate and necessary design choice in 1998. It reflected the political constraints of the peace process and the century-long history of Irish state-building through separation from British security structures.

But that firewall now creates a blind spot.

The security threats facing the shared domain in 2026 are external—Russian hybrid activity, subsea infrastructure attacks, cyber warfare—not internal. These threats were not in the GFA's threat model. They fall outside the firewall's protective scope.

The consequence is an implementation gap. The governance layer—bilateral MoUs, multilateral declarations, working groups—is being established. But there is no implementation mechanism that connects London–Dublin governance to ground-level institutions and agency relationships in Northern Ireland.

Strand Two has a bounded, specific, forward-looking role in filling this gap—not as the governance center, but as the preferred institutional location for implementation coordination on infrastructure-focused security functions. This role respects the firewall's original intent while creating mechanisms for coordinated response to changed threats.

The architecture must be designed for reliability: agency-to-agency protocols as the primary channel, Strand Two as the preferred institutional home, and bilateral arrangements as the fallback if Strand Two becomes non-functional.

The 1998 firewall was necessary and it worked. The question now is whether it can be selectively relaxed.

This paper argues that it can, provided the relaxation is domain-specific, technically grounded, and designed for reliability. The firewall can remain intact as a governance principle—security cooperation is not military alliance, and the GFA's protection of sovereignty and devolved authority remains operative—while operational mechanisms coordinate on external threats.

The firewall between cross-border cooperation and security was a precondition for the GFA's existence. The question now is whether, two decades later, it can be opened for implementation coordination while remaining closed for governance authority.

The answer is yes, if the architecture is designed with clarity, fallbacks, and explicit scope.
