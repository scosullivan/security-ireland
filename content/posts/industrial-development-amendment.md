On 28 April 2026, the Department of Enterprise, Tourism and Employment announced the Industrial Development (Amendment) and Miscellaneous Provisions Bill 2026. The legislation removes a provision in the Industrial Development Act that requires government approval before the IDA, Enterprise Ireland, or other state development agencies can support enterprises engaged in activities of a military or defence-related nature.

![What the Bill changes](/images/charts/ida_bill_changes.png)
*What the Bill changes and what it does not. The legislative reform removes the ministerial gate but does not build the institutional infrastructure behind it.*

## Background

The provision being repealed was originally designed for the now-dissolved Eolas agency — a research body that predates the current enterprise architecture. The government has acknowledged that the restriction is outdated, broad in scope, open to varying interpretations, and creates practical difficulties for agency teams.

The amendment does not create a new policy. It simply removes an institutional obstacle that has prevented Irish enterprise agencies from treating DSR-aligned firms like any other part of the economy. Existing regulatory controls — export licensing, dual-use regulations, EU sanctions compliance — remain fully in force.

## What the Bill does

**Removes the ministerial approval requirement.** Enterprise agencies will no longer need government sign-off to support DSR-aligned firms.

**Expands the IDA's property powers.** The IDA can co-invest in property and infrastructure with third parties, including the Ireland Strategic Investment Fund.

**Enables joint ventures.** The IDA, alone or jointly with Enterprise Ireland, can establish jointly-owned companies with third parties for developing industrial property and infrastructure.

## Does this mean Ireland has a defence industry?

No. The Bill creates legal permission for enterprise agencies to engage with firms operating in the DSR space. The distinction matters.

Ireland has approximately 550 firms active in dual-use technology and a dual-use export market valued at €2.4 billion. Ubotica Technologies uses AI-enabled satellites to track dark vessels. Cathx Ocean's underwater imaging surveys critical seabed infrastructure. Provizio builds sensor systems. VRAI develops VR simulation for defence training. Reamda manufactures bomb disposal robots for the Defence Forces.

What Ireland is not doing — and will not do — is manufacturing fighter jets, tanks, ammunition, or strategic weapons platforms. Minister Burke described the focus as "defence, security and resilience — not military activity."

## What the Bill does not do

The Bill removes a gate. It does not build the road behind it. Several institutional gaps remain:

**No definition of DSR.** The Department lists examples but the Bill establishes no statutory definition. Without one, the same interpretive ambiguity re-emerges.

**No Enterprise Ireland risk assessment framework.** There is no published framework for evaluating reputational risk, export control exposure, or end-use concerns.

**No criteria for agency support decisions.** The old system had a blunt instrument: ministerial sign-off. The new system has no instrument.

**No classified information handling.** Firms in EU defence programmes must handle classified data. Ireland has no industrial classified information infrastructure.

![Five prerequisites](/images/charts/ida_prerequisites.png)
*Progress on the five institutional prerequisites. Only enterprise agency engagement (addressed by the Bill) is complete. Security clearances are partially underway. Three prerequisites have not been started.*

## What remains to be built

The Bill addresses one of five prerequisites. The remaining four require separate action:

1. **Security clearance system** — Interim clearances underway, but must become permanent. Without them, firms cannot receive classified programme data.
2. **Export control framework** — No Munitions List implementation, no end-user certificate system, no re-export controls. Full Wassenaar Arrangement alignment is needed.
3. **Industrial participation policy** — Ireland will spend €2.5bn on defence procurement over the next decade. Without a policy requiring international primes to provide workshare, that money flows abroad with no return.
4. **DSR engagement framework** — Enterprise Ireland and the IDA need: a working DSR definition, risk assessment criteria, staff training, and protocols for distinguishing defensive tech from weapons systems.
