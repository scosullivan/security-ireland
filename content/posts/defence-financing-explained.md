## Where the €800 billion comes from

The €800 billion headline figure is not a single pool of money. It is an aggregation of at least six distinct financing channels, each created by different institutions, funded through different mechanisms, and subject to different conditions.

National sovereign borrowing remains the largest source. When a member state increases its defence budget, the additional spending is typically financed through general government revenue or new borrowing.

EU-level instruments provide a second, smaller channel. SAFE offers €150 billion in EU-backed loans. The European Defence Fund and EDIP provide roughly €10 billion in grants. The EIB has expanded its lending mandate to include direct defence.

Germany's debt brake reform is the single largest national commitment — approximately €200 billion in additional borrowing capacity through the mid-2030s. This is permanent and constitutional, fundamentally different from the temporary fiscal escape clause other states rely on.

Private capital mobilisation is the most speculative channel. The Commission has proposed guarantees that could leverage up to €200 billion. But European defence companies face structural barriers to accessing private capital at scale.

## How sovereign borrowing works

The mechanics of sovereign borrowing for defence are identical to borrowing for any other government function. Each member state has a national debt management office responsible for issuing government bonds.

Defence spending is never earmarked at the point of issuance. When the Finanzagentur auctions a ten-year Bund, the proceeds enter the general federal budget, and the Bundestag's annual budget law determines how much goes to defence.

This is why sovereign creditworthiness is the binding constraint, not the escape clause. Germany can unlock €400 billion because its debt-to-GDP ratio is sixty-two per cent and its bonds carry AAA ratings.

The escape clause changes what governments are allowed to borrow. It does not change what markets are willing to lend.

## How EU-level instruments are created

EU-level defence financing requires a legislative process fundamentally different from national borrowing. Each instrument needs a legal basis in the EU treaties, a proposal from the Commission, and approval through the appropriate Council procedure.

SAFE illustrates the full chain. In March 2025, the Commission proposed a regulation under Article 122 TFEU — the emergency provisions — to create a €150 billion loan facility.

Critically, SAFE loans are debt on member states' balance sheets. The EU borrows cheaply, but the member state must repay. SAFE reduces the cost of borrowing; it does not eliminate the need to repay.

## The impact on capital markets

European defence rearmament is adding tens of billions of euros per year to sovereign bond markets. The more consequential question is not the total volume of new issuance, but where it appears, which sovereigns issue it, at what maturities, and at what credit risk.

Countries already facing elevated borrowing costs — Italy, France, Spain, Belgium — are being asked to increase issuance precisely when fiscal sustainability concerns are most acute.

This creates a potential feedback loop. If investors begin to worry about a country's rising debt, they demand higher interest rates. Higher interest rates mean more spending on debt service, leaving less for defence.

Rearmament is not only a geopolitical event. It is a capital markets event.

## Germany: the full chain

Germany offers the clearest case study of how defence finance works end to end. Five steps trace the journey from constitutional authorisation to delivered capability: constitutional amendment, annual budget allocation, bond issuance, procurement contract, and delivery.

The first Leopard 2A8s are scheduled for 2026; the first F-35As for 2027; Eurofighter Tranche 5 aircraft may not arrive until the 2030s. The timeline from money to capability is measured in years, not months.

## The institutional constraint

The mechanics described in this explainer are the binding constraints on European rearmament — not the political will, which already exists, and not the money, which can be raised. The constraint is the institutional architecture that converts money into capability.

Europe does not have one defence finance system; it has twenty-eight: one for each member state plus the EU's own instruments. Until Europe builds a unified defence finance architecture that effectively pools risk, equalises borrowing costs, and connects capital markets directly to procurement at continental scale, the €800 billion target will remain aspirational.
