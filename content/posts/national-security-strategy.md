## Why Ireland Needs a National Security Strategy

Ireland is one of the few EU member states without a published National Security Strategy. This is not an academic gap. Ireland is chairing the Council of the EU during the largest defence spending programme in European history, operationalising bilateral security cooperation with the United Kingdom for the first time, and attempting to transform a defence establishment at a fifty-year low. Each of these is being managed in isolation. There is no document that explains how these decisions relate to each other, what the state is trying to build, or why.

This paper provides the architecture. A national security strategy must do five things: assess the threat environment honestly, define capability requirements, design the institutional architecture to deliver them, identify the funding model, and specify the cooperation frameworks that extend domestic capacity. This framework does all five — connected as a single system.

The underlying position is precise: Ireland is militarily non-aligned but not neutral to threats. Non-alignment, properly understood, requires more security capability, not less, because a non-aligned state cannot rely on alliance guarantees. Switzerland, Austria, and Finland understood this. Ireland is the exception — a state whose non-alignment has justified the absence of the capability non-alignment demands. This strategy corrects the inversion.

## The Threat Assessment: Five Domains, One Security Environment

Ireland's security has rested on three pillars: invisible threats the public did not perceive, no external pressure to contribute, and a United Kingdom willing to provide a security umbrella without acknowledgement. All three pillars have degraded simultaneously.

The threat environment spans five domains, but they are not five separate problems. They are five dimensions of a single security domain defined by the island and its maritime approaches, and that is institutionally undefended.

![Threat vs Capability Gap Across Five Domains](/images/charts/nss_threat_capability_gap.png)

**Maritime.** Ireland's Exclusive Economic Zone covers 880,000 km² of the northeast Atlantic — one of Europe's largest. Russian submarine activity has increased since 2014. The Naval Service operates nine vessels with no functioning armament and can deploy one large vessel at any time. It has no anti-submarine capability; the UK Royal Navy provides the de facto maritime umbrella with no treaty basis. Surveillance coverage on any given day is estimated at less than fifteen per cent.

**Airspace.** Ireland has no primary radar, no fighter aircraft, no surface-to-air missiles. Under a secret arrangement, the RAF intercepts hostile aircraft — sometimes without prior Irish awareness. Russian military aircraft have been intercepted over Ireland without Irish authorities being initially informed. The airspace is operationally continuous: a radar gap over Ireland degrades situational awareness for the island as a whole.

**Subsea Infrastructure.** Over twenty submarine cables make landfall on Irish shores, carrying over ninety per cent of transatlantic data traffic. The Celtic Interconnector to France arrives in 2027; the Far North Fiber to Alaska, Japan, and Norway is planned for 2026. Ireland is becoming more critical, not less. The Nord Stream sabotage demonstrated that subsea infrastructure is a domain of active hybrid threat. Ireland has no monitoring capability.

**Cyber and Hybrid.** Nine of ten top US tech firms have their EU headquarters in Ireland. A cyber attack on Irish-hosted systems affects the entire Union. The 2021 HSE ransomware attack cost hundreds of millions. During President Zelensky's December 2025 visit, drones were detected seventeen kilometres offshore; Portugal's GNR provided counter-drone defence near Leinster House. Six months before the Presidency, Ireland could not secure its own capital without foreign military assistance.

**Energy.** The island's energy grid operates as a coupled system through the Single Electricity Market. Interconnectors link Ireland to Great Britain, but grid balancing does not respect the border: no joint security protocols exist. Disruption propagates cross-border in real time.

The dependency on the UK for basic security — airspace intercept, maritime patrol, intelligence — is not stable. Brexit introduced friction, and British priorities are shifting to the Indo-Pacific. The RAF's cover is a courtesy, not a treaty obligation. As allegiances are being redrawn, courtesies can be withdrawn.

## Capability Requirements: What Ireland Actually Needs

The threat assessment defines the capability requirements. Ireland does not need to match British or French military capability. It needs to perform four functions.

**Monitor its own domain.** See what is in its airspace, on its seas, under its waters, and in its digital infrastructure. This is the minimum condition for sovereignty; a state that cannot see what is happening in its own territory is outsourcing sovereignty.

**Respond to sub-threshold incidents.** Counter-drone, cyber incident response, cable coordination, energy infrastructure protection: these are the actual threats, none of which requires a standing army of fifty thousand. But they all require institutional capacity that Ireland currently lacks.

**Contribute in its geographic domains.** Maritime surveillance, subsea protection, cyber resilience: Ireland does not need main battle tanks. It needs to contribute where its geography makes its contribution irreplaceable.

**Sustain capability over time.** Recruit and retain personnel, maintain equipment, build procurement expertise, and develop intelligence architecture. This is the institutional dimension that spending alone cannot provide.

The capability framework maps these four functions across all five threat domains, from the current Level of Ambition (LOA 1, which is inadequate) through LOA 2 (monitoring capability, target 2028) to LOA 3 (minimum for self-defence). The key platforms include primary radar by 2028, counter-drone systems by 2026, CISE maritime data sharing, armed naval vessels with anti-submarine warfare capability, NCSC strengthening, and unified intelligence assessment.

## Institutional Architecture: Five Reforms

Ireland ran a €42 billion surplus between 2022 and 2024. It could fund LOA 3 from a single year's surplus. The constraint is not money but institutional bandwidth: the distance between what the system can coordinate and what the security environment demands. Ireland's system has been architecturally configured for the absence of military capability since the disbandment of the National Army in 1923.

![Institutional Reform Architecture](/images/charts/nss_institutional_reforms.png)

**National Security Council.** Ireland's National Security Committee exists but lacks institutional weight. This strategy proposes a National Security Council chaired by the Taoiseach with statutory authority over threat assessment, capability planning, and interagency coordination. The NSC is the institutional home for this strategy — the body responsible for its implementation and review.

**Unified Intelligence.** Ireland's intelligence is split between J2/IMIS (military), Garda CSB (police), and NCSC (cyber) — unique in Europe. Every comparable state has unified intelligence. Without fused threat assessment, every system — radar, maritime surveillance, cyber — operates in isolation. Intelligence reform is the prerequisite for everything else.

**Transformed Procurement.** Ireland's procurement has managed routine replacement. It is now delivering primary radar, counter-drone systems, naval armament, and armoured vehicles — capabilities nobody in the system has operated. This requires dedicated programme management, external technical expertise, and integration planning for systems that do not yet exist.

**Competitive Personnel Architecture.** 7,531 against an establishment of 9,739 means Ireland is at a fifty-year low. A Private 3 Star salary is €40,000 where rent near barracks exceeds €1,800 per month. This strategy proposes targeted compensation reform for specialist technical roles, housing support linked to barracks locations, and a reformed reserve drawing on civilian expertise.

**Defence Industrial Policy.** Ireland has €2.5 billion in emerging procurement demand and no industrial policy. This strategy establishes that Ireland's contribution is domain-specific: cyber, maritime sensing, AI, and dual-use technology. The institutional prerequisites — security clearances, classified information handling, Enterprise Ireland engagement, export controls — must be built in parallel with the procurement pipeline.

## The Funding Model: Five Channels as One Architecture

Ireland's defence funding flows through five channels. This strategy connects them as a single architecture.

![Defence Funding Channels](/images/charts/nss_funding_channels.png)

**National budget:** The €1.7 billion capital programme over 2026–2030 funds radar, naval capability, counter-drone systems, vehicles, and personnel. This strategy proposes a statutory defence investment floor — expressed as a percentage of GNI* rather than GDP, to account for Ireland's distorted national accounts — that insulates the programme from annual budget politics. GNI* is the CSO's adjusted measure of actual national income. A floor pegged to GNI* would be analytically honest and practically enforceable.

**EU instruments:** SAFE (€150 billion in loans), EDIP (€1.5 billion in grants), and EDF (€8 billion in R&D). The Presidency determines whether Ireland can access these. The four Presidency Desk briefs are designed to ensure the rules recognise domain-specific contribution.

**Bilateral UK–Ireland:** The Cork MoU enables joint procurement, government-to-government sales, and personnel exchanges. This strategy treats bilateral cooperation as an efficiency multiplier.

**NATO-adjacent:** ITPP, North Sea Declaration. Standards, exercises, intelligence access. Non-aligned but networked — the Swiss model.

**CISE and data:** Maritime surveillance sharing. Ireland as data provider through an Atlantic node, not consumer.

## The Cooperation Architecture: Three Tracks

Ireland's security cannot be delivered domestically alone. The strategy operates through three cooperation tracks that feed into each other.

![Three-Track Cooperation Architecture](/images/charts/nss_three_tracks.png)

**Track 1 — Domestic:** Build the institutional infrastructure — radar, naval, cyber, intelligence, personnel, procurement — that gives Ireland something to contribute. Without domestic capacity, cooperation frameworks are empty.

**Track 2 — Bilateral:** Extend domestic capacity into the shared UK–Ireland security domain. Joint procurement, exercises, intelligence-sharing. The bilateral track demonstrates to EU partners that Ireland generates real capability. The Cork MoU is the institutional vehicle.

**Track 3 — EU:** Institutionalise Ireland's contribution within EU governance and funding. PESCO formalises the contribution. EDIP and SAFE fund it. The MFF makes it permanent. The Presidency locks it in.

This is the strategy's central structural insight: the three tracks are not alternatives, they are layers of a single architecture. Each layer builds on the one before it. The bilateral track builds the institutional capacity that makes the EU track credible.

## Implementation Pathway

![Implementation Phases](/images/charts/nss_implementation_phases.png)

**Phase 1 — Immediate (2026).** Publish this strategy. Establish the National Security Council. Complete counter-drone procurement. Execute the four Presidency Desk briefs. Begin UK live exercises in September. Apply for SAFE loans.

**Phase 2 — Near-term (2027–2028).** Deliver primary radar. Achieve CISE node operational capability. Legislate the statutory defence investment floor. Begin intelligence unification. Implement defence industrial policy. Commission first new-build naval vessel.

**Phase 3 — Medium-term (2028–2030).** Achieve LOA 2 across all domains. Unified intelligence agency operational. First domestic cyber and maritime sensing contracts within EU frameworks. Begin LOA 3 planning.

**Phase 4 — Strategic horizon (2030–2035).** LOA 3 capability development. Permanent EU funding pathways operational. Mature defence industrial participation. Intelligence fully integrated with partners. Strategy review and update.

## What Happens Without This Strategy

Without a national security strategy, Ireland continues making consequential decisions in isolation. The Cork MoU, the radar programme, the counter-drone procurement, the Presidency briefs, the PESCO projects — each proceeds without a framework explaining how they relate. The €1.7 billion programme produces equipment without the institutional architecture to convert it into capability; the EU Presidency passes without locking in Ireland's permanent role. And Ireland's security continues to rest on a dependency it cannot acknowledge, provided by a partner it defined itself in opposition to, under an arrangement that has no treaty basis.

Capital being spent without institutional architecture does not produce capabilities — it produces expenditure. Without a strategy, Ireland's defence transformation becomes a collection of procurement transactions rather than the construction of a national security system.
