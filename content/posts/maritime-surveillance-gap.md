## The Strategic Challenge

Ireland's maritime territory comprises one of the largest exclusive economic zones (EEZ) in Europe, extending 200 nautical miles from the coast and encompassing approximately 900,000 square kilometres. This vast area presents enormous economic opportunities—from fisheries to renewable energy—but also significant security and governance challenges. Despite these opportunities, Ireland's ability to monitor, detect, and respond to maritime activity remains constrained by decades of underinvestment.

The COVID-19 pandemic starkly exposed this vulnerability when Irish coastal waters experienced significant illegal fishing activity with minimal capacity to respond. More concerning, the lack of comprehensive maritime awareness creates blind spots for law enforcement, environmental protection, and national security.

## Current Capabilities and Constraints

The Irish Naval Service operates a fleet of offshore and coastal patrol vessels, primarily the Oileán Gheálach-class and Eithne-class vessels. These ships provide visible maritime presence but are insufficient for continuous surveillance of Ireland's EEZ. They are supplemented by maritime patrol aircraft and occasional cooperation with other European navies, but systematic, comprehensive coverage remains elusive.

The primary constraint is financial. Maritime surveillance requires either substantial vessel flotillas conducting regular patrols or advanced technology systems capable of persistent monitoring. Ireland currently possesses neither in sufficient measure. The budget for naval operations has historically been limited, reflecting political choices and competing fiscal priorities.

## Technical Solutions and Options

Several technological approaches could substantially improve maritime awareness:

**Autonomous Surface Vehicles (ASVs)**: Uncrewed surface vessels can operate for extended periods, conducting surveillance and gathering intelligence at lower operational cost than crewed vessels. Several European countries have begun pilot programs with promising results.

**Satellite-Based Monitoring**: Enhanced partnerships with European satellite programs and emerging commercial satellite capabilities offer potential for persistent, cost-effective coverage. The EU's Copernicus program already provides data; Ireland could expand utilization of these systems.

**Integrated Maritime Domain Awareness**: Comprehensive systems integrating vessel automatic identification systems (AIS), naval radar, cooperative information sharing with neighbouring states, and environmental monitoring could provide real-time situational awareness across the EEZ.

## Recommendations

Ireland should pursue a three-tier approach: maintenance of core naval presence, expanded use of emerging technologies, and enhanced cooperation with European partners through PESCO maritime initiatives. Investment in maritime surveillance technology would yield returns across fisheries management, environmental protection, and security.
