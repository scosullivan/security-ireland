## The Paradox: Industrial Capacity Without Defence Industrialisation

Ireland possesses the ingredients for defence industrial development: a global electronics and software manufacturing base, precision engineering expertise, world-class research institutions, and deep integration within EU industrial networks. Yet Ireland has no defence industrial base. Defence manufacturing contributes less than 1% of Irish manufacturing output. No major defence contractor operates a primary facility in Ireland. Irish firms are invisible in European defence supply chains.

This paradox is not accidental. It reflects political choice, legislative restrictions, and institutional architecture rather than lack of industrial capability. Understanding Ireland's defence industrial potential therefore requires understanding not what Ireland cannot make, but what policy choices and institutional structures have prevented industrial firms from making it.

The starting point is recognising what already exists: Ireland is not starting from zero. Approximately 550 Irish firms operate in dual-use sectors—technologies with both civilian and defence applications. These firms employ approximately 40,000 people and generate approximately €2.4 billion in annual export revenue. This is the foundation upon which a defence industrial base could be built. The question is not whether Ireland has the industrial capacity. It is whether institutional architecture will be constructed to enable that capacity to participate in defence markets.

## The Dual-Use Technology Base

Dual-use technology encompasses a wide range of sectors: electronics manufacturing, software development, marine engineering, telecommunications, materials science, and logistics. Ireland's presence in these sectors is substantial:

- **Electronics and semiconductor manufacturing**: Ireland hosts manufacturing and design operations for companies like Intel, Apple, and others. While most output is civilian (processors, components), the technical capabilities—precision fabrication, quality control, supply chain management—are directly transferable to defence applications.

- **Software and cybersecurity**: Irish software firms are world-leading in areas like cloud computing, security protocols, and artificial intelligence. These capabilities have direct defence applications in cyber warfare, command-and-control systems, and intelligence analysis.

- **Marine and subsea technology**: Ireland's maritime engineering base, concentrated in Cork and other coastal regions, includes firms specialising in subsea robotics, underwater imaging, and marine sensor systems. These are dual-use technologies with immediate naval applications.

- **Telecommunications and sensor systems**: Irish telecom and sensor manufacturers serve global markets. The technical sophistication required for civilian-grade sensor networks transfers directly to military applications.

- **Logistics and supply chain systems**: Irish firms operate sophisticated logistics networks, inventory management, and supply chain optimisation systems. These are critical for defence industrial coordination but operate entirely in civilian markets.

Aggregating across these sectors, approximately €2.4 billion in dual-use technology exports flows from Ireland annually. This is a meaningful industrial base, substantially larger than the defence spending of Ireland, Denmark, or Luxembourg. The critical insight is that this industrial capacity exists. It is not latent or potential. It generates revenue, employs skilled workers, and maintains global market presence. The constraint is not whether Irish firms can manufacture defence-relevant technology. It is whether they are permitted to, and whether the institutional framework exists to enable them to do so.

## Five Institutional Prerequisites

Defence industrialisation is not a straightforward scaling of civilian manufacturing. It requires five institutional prerequisites, each of which is necessary but not sufficient:

### Prerequisite 1: Procurement Demand

The first prerequisite is demand. A defence firm cannot manufacture weapons systems if no government is buying. Irish firms cannot become defence suppliers if the Irish Defence Forces procure so little equipment that no firm can justify specialising in defence. This is the baseline constraint.

Ireland's €1.24 billion annual defence budget, of which only €350 million is capital, creates limited procurement demand. By comparison, Denmark's defence budget of €5 billion, with capital spending of €1.5–2 billion annually, creates sufficient demand to support a domestic defence industrial ecosystem. Poland's defence budget of €15 billion creates enormous demand pull, attracting foreign suppliers and enabling domestic industrial development.

The government's €1.7 billion capital plan (2026–2030) represents a modest expansion of procurement demand. However, a capital budget of €340 million annually remains small. For context, a single procurement programme (a new frigate class for the Navy, or a new transport aircraft) typically costs €500 million to €1 billion. The Irish capital budget supports only two to three such programmes across a five-year period.

The demand problem is therefore structural. Ireland cannot single-handedly create demand sufficient to support a defence industrial base. The solution is two-fold: increase national procurement demand, and enable Irish firms to participate in European defence procurement through EU frameworks (EDIP, PESCO programmes, defence technology cooperation agreements).

### Prerequisite 2: Security Clearances

As noted in previous analysis, Ireland lacks a security clearance system. This is a hard institutional prerequisite. Without the ability to clear personnel and facilities to handle classified information, Irish firms cannot participate in classified defence programmes. And most defence programmes are classified, at least partially.

The government has moved to address this through interim measures: temporary security clearance protocols for EDF and EDIP participation, beginning in late 2024. However, permanent security clearance infrastructure requires legislation, institutional capacity, and reciprocal arrangements with NATO and EU partners. The Security and Intelligence Act, currently in legislative development, will formalise this when enacted (target: late 2026 or early 2027). A vetting authority with statutory powers will be established (target: 2027).

Until this infrastructure is operational, Irish firms are constrained to unclassified or low-classification defence work. This is not trivial—unclassified technology development still adds value—but it creates a ceiling on industrial participation. Most strategic defence programmes cannot accommodate unclassified participation.

### Prerequisite 3: Export Controls

Irish firms currently operate under dual-use export control regulations inherited from EU frameworks. These regulations require export licences for certain technologies (semiconductors above certain specifications, encryption software, advanced materials). However, Irish firms have no domestic export control authority responsible for evaluating defence-specific exports.

When an Irish firm seeks to export defence-related technology, the request is evaluated under general dual-use controls but without a defence-specific assessment process. This creates regulatory ambiguity: is the technology restricted? Is the end-use permissible? What is the government's strategic interest in this export?

A defence-specific export control regime requires legislation establishing a licensing authority, criteria for export decisions, and interagency coordination (Defence, Foreign Affairs, Business, Science). This does not constrain exports—properly designed, it streamlines them by providing clear criteria and predictable timelines. However, it has not been established.

### Prerequisite 4: Industrial Participation Policy

As discussed in previous analysis, Ireland lacks an explicit industrial participation requirement in defence procurement. This is not a statutory barrier but a policy choice. When the Irish Defence Forces procure equipment, no requirement mandates that a portion of the contract flow to Irish subcontractors or that technology transfer occur.

Establishing industrial participation policy requires policy development and legislative codification. Typical industrial participation requirements range from 15% to 30% of contract value flowing to domestic or strategic suppliers. This is embedded in procurement regulations as a contract requirement, typically negotiated during tender evaluation.

Ireland has not done this. The Defence Industrial Policy Framework (DIPF), in development as of March 2026, is intended to establish these requirements. However, until DIPF is operationalised (target: 2027), industrial participation remains discretionary rather than systematic.

### Prerequisite 5: Political Economy and Industrial Policy

The deepest prerequisite is political economy: a government commitment to defence industrialisation as an economic and security objective. Defence industrialisation is not a market outcome. It requires deliberate policy choices that may conflict with immediate fiscal objectives or create political tension.

For example, establishing industrial participation requirements in defence procurement typically raises procurement costs by 10–15% compared to lowest-bidder tendering. A €100 million procurement with 20% industrial participation costs approximately €105–110 million instead of €100 million. This is deliberate inefficiency—paying more to build domestic industrial capacity rather than minimising immediate cost.

Similarly, restricting defence-relevant exports to secure partnerships and advance foreign policy objectives means leaving money on the table. A software security firm that could export to any country instead restricts exports to NATO-aligned partners, sacrificing some commercial opportunity for strategic alignment.

These political economy choices require political consensus. They must survive electoral cycles. And they require government capacity to implement: industrial policy requires sophisticated interagency coordination, which Ireland has not traditionally invested in for defence sectors.

## Where Ireland Stands: Progress on Institutional Prerequisites

As of March 2026, the government has moved meaningfully on two prerequisites and less so on three:

**Moved on: Security Clearances and Export Controls**
The government has established interim security clearance protocols (2024), begun drafting the Security and Intelligence Act (target completion: late 2026), and committed to establishing a permanent vetting authority. This is genuine progress. Similarly, discussions are underway about establishing a defence-specific export control regime within a broader dual-use export control framework. These moves are slow but directional.

**Not yet moved: Procurement demand, industrial participation policy, and political economy consensus**
The €1.7 billion capital plan increases procurement demand modestly but does not yet create structural demand pull for defence industrial specialisation. Industrial participation policy exists as a draft framework but has not been legislated or operationalised. And political consensus about defending defence industrialisation as a policy objective remains limited. Most Irish political parties support increased defence spending abstractly, but few have articulated a vision of defence industrialisation as an economic development strategy.

## Ireland's Bounded Role: What Ireland Should Industrialise

A critical question emerges: should Ireland attempt to develop a full defence industrial base, or should it focus on specific technologies where Irish capabilities are strongest?

The answer is bounded role. Ireland should not aspire to produce fighter jets, main battle tanks, or strategic missiles. These require industrial ecosystems—supply chains, skilled labour pools, capital investments, and export markets—that only large nations can sustain. Instead, Ireland's bounded role should focus on areas where Irish capabilities are strongest: maritime AI and autonomous systems, cyber technology and security, subsea monitoring and robotics, and dual-use sensing systems.

Maritime AI and autonomous systems are areas where Irish software and robotics firms have demonstrable capabilities. Development of autonomous surface vessels (ASVs) for coastal surveillance, underwater drones for subsea monitoring, and AI systems for maritime data processing are all dual-use technologies where Irish firms are globally competitive.

Cyber technology and security are areas where Irish software firms already operate at world-leading levels. Translating civilian cybersecurity expertise into defence cyber capabilities—industrial control systems security, critical infrastructure protection, military network defence—requires security clearances and classified programme access, but not new technological capability.

Subsea monitoring and robotics draw on Irish marine engineering expertise concentrated in Cork and other coastal regions. Sonar systems, underwater cameras, cable-laying robotics, and subsea infrastructure inspection technologies are all areas where Irish engineering firms have competitive advantage.

Dual-use sensing systems—synthetic aperture radar, hyperspectral imaging, thermal sensing—are technologies where Irish electronics and software firms can compete. These sensors have civilian applications (environmental monitoring, resource surveying) and defence applications (surveillance, reconnaissance), creating natural pathways for commercial development with defence applications.

## The EU Framework: Industrial Participation Through EDIP and PESCO

The practical pathway for Irish defence industrialisation is not building a sovereign Irish defence industry. It is enabling Irish firms to participate in European defence industrial cooperation through EU frameworks: the European Defence Industrial Programme (EDIP), PESCO (Permanent Structured Cooperation) defence technology projects, and bilateral defence technology cooperation with larger EU members.

EDIP provides grant funding for defence industrial capacity building, technology development, and supply chain integration. Irish firms can apply for EDIP funding to develop maritime AI systems, cyber security technologies, or subsea monitoring equipment in partnership with European contractors. This approach reduces technology risk (shared investment with European partners), creates scale (European markets), and builds Irish capacity within a broader European industrial architecture.

PESCO defence technology projects are structured cooperative arrangements between EU members focused on specific technologies: for example, a maritime surveillance project, a cyber security project, or a logistics system project. Ireland can participate in PESCO projects in areas aligned with Irish capabilities, contributing technology development while gaining market access to other participating states.

This is the realistic pathway. Ireland will not produce complete defence systems independently. But Irish firms can become specialised suppliers within European defence industrial networks, developing particular technologies (AI, cyber, sensors) that are integrated into larger systems developed and manufactured by larger European firms. This pathway generates industrial development, skilled employment, and defence capability contribution without requiring Ireland to sustain a complete defence industrial ecosystem.

## Timeline and Implementation

The institutional prerequisites are being built on overlapping timelines:

- **Security clearances**: Interim protocols operational (2024), permanent framework legislation late 2026, operational vetting authority by 2027
- **Export controls**: Discussions ongoing, legislative framework anticipated late 2026, operational regime by 2027–2028
- **Industrial participation policy**: Draft Defence Industrial Policy Framework (2026), legislative codification and operationalisation by 2027
- **Procurement demand**: €1.7 billion capital plan (2026–2030) provides baseline demand; expansion contingent on future political decisions
- **Political economy**: Gradual consensus-building through EU Presidency and Strategic Compass implementation; no dramatic shift anticipated

The timeline is realistic. Each prerequisite requires legislation, institutional capacity-building, and coordination with EU and international partners. Trying to accelerate would create implementation risk. The government's approach—sequential, measured, with adequate time for institutional development—is appropriate.

## Industrial Potential and Strategic Value

Ireland's defence industrial role will be modest relative to France, Germany, or the UK. However, it will be meaningful. A mature Irish defence industrial base, focused on maritime AI, cyber technology, subsea monitoring, and sensing systems, could generate €200–300 million in annual defence-related exports and sustain 1,500–2,500 industrial jobs. This is not transformative for the Irish economy but is meaningful for sectors concentrated in coastal regions and for technology firms seeking to diversify revenue streams.

More importantly, industrial participation in European defence cooperation generates security benefits: interoperability with European systems, participation in technology development, and integration into European defence industrial networks. From a security perspective, an Irish firm embedded in European supply chains is more valuable than a hypothetical fully sovereign (and tiny) Irish defence firm.

The constraint is not whether Ireland can industrialise defence-relevant technology. The constraint is whether the government will build the institutional architecture to enable it. The current trajectory—moving on security clearances and export controls while developing industrial participation policy—suggests the government intends to build that architecture. The outcome depends on whether this commitment survives the inevitable political cycles and competing priorities that lie ahead.
