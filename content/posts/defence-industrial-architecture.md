## The Argument

Ireland possesses significant technological assets for defence production yet lacks the institutional architecture to convert those assets into a functioning defence industrial base. Approximately 550 dual-use technology firms operate in Ireland, generating a €2.4 billion dual-use export market. The country hosts the European headquarters of nine of the ten largest US technology companies, creating an extraordinary concentration of engineering talent, R&D capability, and manufacturing precision in sectors directly relevant to modern defence production.

Ireland sits at the intersection of three critical domains where EU defence spending is increasingly concentrated: maritime technology, subsea systems, and cyber capabilities. European defence frameworks are allocating tens of billions into these sectors, creating opportunities for Irish firms to participate in high-value supply chains and capability development. Yet Irish companies face systematic barriers that prevent them from accessing these opportunities.

The first barrier is the absence of a security clearance system. Without domestic personnel and facility security clearances, Irish firms cannot participate in classified EU defence programmes. The second is the lack of an industrial participation policy—meaning Irish domestic procurement does not generate supply chain positions for Irish firms. Third is the absence of export controls aligned with international regimes, preventing independent defence technology exports. Fourth is the lack of political economy infrastructure—no industrial strategy, no innovation coordination, no enterprise development within the defence sector. Fifth is the absence of procurement demand itself—Ireland has only recently begun substantial defence procurement.

None of these gaps are technological. All reflect institutional architecture decisions that other European states have made and that Ireland could make.

## The Current Industrial Base

Ireland's defence and dual-use technology sector is substantial but fragmented. The 550 active firms span precision manufacturing, advanced materials, software engineering, systems integration, and sensor development. The €2.4 billion dual-use export market reflects capability across sectors with direct defence application: electronics, communications systems, advanced manufacturing processes, and materials science.

What distinguishes Ireland's industrial base is not the existence of individual firms but the depth of the engineering talent pool. The presence of nine major US technology company headquarters has created a concentration of PhDs, senior engineers, and R&D leaders with experience in products and processes of immediate defence relevance. This talent concentration is unusual in European terms—the primary competitive advantage Ireland possesses.

However, this advantage is currently atomised. Firms operate independently, often unaware of potential collaboration opportunities with other Irish companies. There is no domestic defence procurement to drive cluster formation. There is no industrial strategy providing visibility to firms about future demand. There is no infrastructure for security clearances, meaning firms cannot even bid for the most lucrative opportunities in EU defence programmes.

## The Five Missing Prerequisites

The architecture gap between Ireland's technological capacity and its institutional readiness comprises five interlocking prerequisites that must be established:

**1. Procurement Demand**

Defence industrialisation is demand-driven. An industrial base emerges when a state commits to sustained procurement of domestically-sourced capabilities. Ireland now has this: the €1.7 billion capital plan for 2026-2030, the €1.5 billion budget commitment for 2026, and the commitment to reach Level of Ambition 2 by 2028 creates a multi-year procurement pipeline.

The challenge is translating this pipeline into visible industrial participation requirements. Other European states explicitly write industrial participation conditions into their procurement—mandating that a percentage of value be sourced domestically or from EU suppliers. Ireland has not yet adopted this approach systematically.

**2. Security Clearances**

Every defence-procuring state in Europe operates a security clearance system for personnel and facilities. This system enables firms to handle classified information and participate in classified programmes. Ireland has none. The Irish government is currently developing interim arrangements and has indicated movement toward establishing a formal clearance system, but this remains incomplete.

Without clearances, Irish firms are excluded from the most substantive EU defence programmes—those involving classified capability development, intelligence applications, or classified procurement requirements. The scale of this exclusion is significant. A large portion of EU defence funding flows through classified channels.

**3. Export Controls**

Ireland has export controls for conventional weapons (via EU law) but lacks comprehensive export control legislation aligned with international regimes for defence technology and dual-use goods. This creates two problems. First, firms cannot independently export defence technology without a legislative framework. Second, when Irish firms participate in EU programmes, questions arise about the treatment of technical data and intellectual property generated through that participation.

The Irish government is moving toward implementing export control legislation aligned with international regimes, but this has not yet been enacted.

**4. Industrial Participation Policy**

Ireland does not have an industrial participation or offset policy for defence procurement. These policies typically require that a percentage of defence procurement value be sourced domestically or from specified partners (EU, NATO). They create visibility for firms about future demand and direct procurement toward domestic suppliers.

Industrial participation is not the main mechanism driving defence industrial development—that is driven by genuine capability demand and technology advantage. But it provides a transparent mechanism for converting procurement into industrial opportunity, and it signals to the market that domestic supply chains matter.

**5. Political Economy and Industrial Strategy**

Finally, Ireland lacks an explicit defence industrial strategy that provides firms with visibility about future demand, policy support for clustering and collaboration, or coordination mechanisms with government and EU institutions.

## What Success Looks Like: Ireland's Bounded Role

Ireland will not become an independent arms manufacturer. It will not develop a domestic missile, aircraft, or ship-building industry. These sectors require capital scales and production volumes that would never be cost-effective for Ireland.

Ireland's bounded role within European defence production consists of four domains:

**Maritime AI and Autonomy**: Ireland has significant capability in artificial intelligence, autonomous systems, and real-time data processing. These capabilities are directly applicable to autonomous maritime platforms—uncrewed surface and subsea vehicles for surveillance, environmental monitoring, and intelligence gathering. EU funding for autonomous maritime systems is substantial, and Irish participation could be significant.

**Cyber Technology**: Irish firms have deep capability in cyber security, network defence, and encryption. EU cyber defence programmes require sustained industrial participation. Irish firms could supply both defensive systems and security services to EU programmes.

**Subsea Monitoring and Communications**: Ireland has significant offshore engineering capability (derived from the oil and gas sector) in subsea systems, deepwater communications, and marine sensing. These capabilities are directly applicable to European maritime surveillance infrastructure.

**Dual-Use Sensing and Electronics**: Sensor development, advanced electronics, and precision manufacturing processes developed in Irish dual-use industry can contribute to EU defence programmes without requiring Ireland to develop independent defence industrial capacity.

These four domains represent not a large share of European defence spending but a high-value, technology-intensive share that aligns with Ireland's existing capabilities and could generate substantial economic value while meeting genuine defence requirements.

## The Institutional Path Forward

The government is actively addressing two of the five prerequisites: security clearances and S&T Act restrictions. What remains unaddressed are procurement demand signalling, industrial participation policy, and explicit industrial strategy.

The path forward requires three moves:

First, enact security clearance legislation and establish the institutional infrastructure (a clearance authority, trained assessors, facility certification processes). The government has indicated movement in this direction, and legislation should follow within 12 months.

Second, establish an industrial participation requirement for Irish defence procurement, beginning with the €1.7 billion capital plan. This provides visibility to firms about supply chain opportunities and signals that Irish industrial participation matters. This can be implemented through Defence Procurement Procedure guidance without legislation.

Third, develop an explicit defence industrial strategy that coordinates government procurement, EU participation strategy, R&D support, and firm clustering and collaboration. This strategy should be housed in the Defence Industrial Authority or equivalent body, sitting at the intersection of government procurement, EU policy, and innovation support.

## Implications

Ireland possesses all the technological and human capital inputs required to develop a functioning defence industrial base. What it lacks is the institutional architecture that converts those inputs into sustained industrial activity and supply chain participation.

This architecture is not technically complex. Every defence-procuring state in Europe has built similar systems. The cost is modest—the institutional investment required is measured in tens of millions, not billions. The time horizon is medium-term—most of the required architecture can be established within 18-24 months.

The payoff is significant. A functioning defence industrial base creates high-value employment, generates export revenue, strengthens European defence capabilities, and establishes Ireland as a technology partner within European defence frameworks. This is a coordination problem with a clear solution and substantial upside.

The time for architecture building is now, before the window of EU defence investment and industrial opportunity narrows.
