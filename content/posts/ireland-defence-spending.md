## Introduction: The Context for Irish Defence Spending

Ireland's defence spending has historically been among the lowest in Europe, both in absolute terms and as a percentage of GDP. This reflects historical policy choices rooted in neutrality commitments, a post-Cold War assumption of benign strategic environment, and political prioritization of other government spending. Ireland's 1.24 billion euro annual defence budget, while substantial in absolute terms, represents just 0.22 per cent of GDP—the lowest proportion among EU member states and well below NATO targets of 2 per cent for member states.

This historical under-investment in defence has created consequences visible in contemporary capability gaps. The Irish Defence Forces operate equipment from the 1970s and 1980s. Air defence capability is absent. Anti-submarine capability is limited. Maritime surveillance cannot cover the Irish EEZ. Personnel strength is at fifty-year lows. These capability gaps are not primarily the result of poor spending decisions but of decades of under-investment relative to the requirement.

In recent years, strategic environment change has forced reconsideration of Irish defence investment. The 2022 Russian invasion of Ukraine shattered assumptions about European security. Heightened Russian naval activity in European waters creates new security realities. EU strategic autonomy discussions have highlighted the importance of member state defence capability. The emergence of hybrid threats and cyber attacks has created new security challenges. Against this backdrop, the Irish government has committed to increased defence spending, with budget records in 2025 and 2026, and a €1.7 billion capital plan for 2026-2030.

Yet even with these increases, fundamental questions remain about adequacy. Is current spending sufficient to achieve the government's stated Levels of Ambition? What would funding requirements look like for a fully autonomous Irish defence capability? How does Irish spending compare to peer nations with similar population and GDP? What is the composition of Irish defence spending and is it optimal? This paper addresses these questions, providing context for understanding Irish defence spending in international comparison and providing framework for evaluating the adequacy of current and projected spending.

## International Comparison: Ireland's Spending in Context

### NATO/EU Benchmark Comparisons

NATO member states have committed to defence spending targets of 2 per cent of GDP by 2024. This target emerged from repeated NATO summits and represents the alliance's assessment of adequate spending for collective defence. The 2 per cent standard has become the primary metric for evaluating defence spending adequacy internationally.

Ireland, as a non-NATO member, is not bound by the 2 per cent target. However, the target provides useful international benchmark. Ireland's current spending of 0.22 per cent of GDP is roughly one-ninth of the NATO benchmark. Even with the committed increases (reaching approximately 0.3 per cent of GDP by 2026-2027), Ireland will remain well below NATO benchmarks.

EU member states that are NATO members face pressure to meet the 2 per cent target. Among EU members, spending levels vary:

- Germany: approximately 2.0-2.5 per cent of GDP (€80+ billion annually)
- France: approximately 1.8-1.9 per cent of GDP (€50+ billion)
- Poland: approximately 2.4 per cent of GDP (€15+ billion)
- Spain: approximately 1.3 per cent of GDP (€15+ billion)
- Italy: approximately 1.5 per cent of GDP (€28+ billion)
- Netherlands: approximately 2.0 per cent of GDP (€14+ billion)
- Ireland: approximately 0.22 per cent of GDP (€1.24 billion)

Ireland's spending proportion is the lowest in the EU, and significantly below peers with comparable population and economic development.

### Peer Nation Comparisons

Comparing Ireland to peer nations with similar population and GDP provides additional context:

**Portugal** (similar population, similar GDP): Spends approximately €4 billion annually (1.5-1.6 per cent of GDP). While Portugal is a NATO member, the comparison is instructive. Portugal's spending is more than three times Ireland's (absolute) and seven times as high (as per cent of GDP).

**Greece** (similar population, somewhat higher GDP): Spends approximately €6.5 billion annually (about 2.5-3 per cent of GDP). This elevated spending reflects NATO membership and specific security concerns (Turkey, instability in Eastern Mediterranean).

**Belgium** (similar population, similar GDP): Spends approximately €4.5 billion annually (1.1-1.2 per cent of GDP). Despite being smaller than Ireland's GDP, Belgium spends roughly 3.6 times what Ireland spends.

**Denmark** (similar population, similar GDP): Spends approximately €5 billion annually (1.4-1.5 per cent of GDP). More than four times Ireland's spending.

**Sweden** (pre-NATO, but recently joined): Has been spending 1.5-2.0 per cent of GDP on defence, and has committed to increase toward 2.5 per cent post-NATO membership.

These comparisons reveal a consistent pattern: Ireland's defence spending is substantially lower than comparable nations, both in absolute terms and relative to GDP or economic capacity.

### Adjusted International Comparison

One argument sometimes made is that Ireland's spending is adequate because Ireland faces limited external military threat. This argument must be examined carefully. While Ireland may face lower direct military threat than some nations (particularly those bordering Russia or in contested regions), several factors suggest that this argument underestimates adequate spending:

**Strategic Uncertainty**: Current strategic environment is characterized by increasing uncertainty. Russia's invasion of Ukraine overturned assumptions about European security that had prevailed for decades. Strategic environment could change rapidly, requiring enhanced capability.

**Critical Infrastructure Vulnerability**: Ireland's maritime zones contain critical infrastructure (submarine cables, potential energy infrastructure) that requires protection and surveillance. This is not a threat in the traditional sense but a security requirement.

**EU Integration and Burden-Sharing**: As Ireland deepens participation in European security frameworks and contributes to European security, spending adequacy should be measured partly by Ireland's contribution to collective European security, not just by direct Irish security threats.

**Small-State Vulnerability**: Small states without credible defence capability are more vulnerable to coercion and pressure. Developing credible defence capability is a national interest independent of specific threat assessment.

**Economic Benefits**: Defence spending generates economic benefits through employment, innovation, and industrial development. These benefits can justify spending levels beyond minimal security requirements.

Given these factors, an argument can be made that Ireland's spending should be comparable to peer nations—in the 1.0-1.5 per cent of GDP range—rather than at current 0.22 per cent levels.

## Composition of Irish Defence Spending

Understanding Irish defence spending requires examining how the €1.24 billion budget is allocated. The allocation reveals important constraints and implications:

### Personnel Costs

Approximately 58 per cent of the Irish defence budget (€720 million) goes to personnel costs—salaries, benefits, pension contributions, and training for Irish Defence Forces personnel. With approximately 9,000 personnel, this yields per-soldier cost of approximately €80,000 annually. By international comparison, this is in the mid-range; some countries' per-soldier costs are lower, others higher.

The dominance of personnel costs creates several constraints:

**Limited Capital Available**: With only 42 per cent of budget (approximately €520 million) available for capital expenditure, operations, and support functions, there is limited capacity for major equipment purchases or modernization.

**Personnel Growth Challenges**: Growing the force from 9,000 to 10,500 (LOA 2 requirement) or to 13,000 (LOA 3 requirement) would increase personnel costs by roughly €120 million per 1,000 personnel. This limits capital available for equipment.

**Efficiency Pressures**: The high share of personnel costs creates pressure to improve efficiency in personnel management, training, and force structure to free resources for capital spending.

### Capital Expenditure

Approximately 30 per cent of the defence budget (approximately €370 million) is allocated to capital expenditure—equipment purchases, facility modernization, infrastructure investment. This capital budget is insufficient for the scale of modernization required.

The capital plan announced for 2026-2030 (€1.7 billion total, or approximately €340 million annually) represents doubling of the typical annual capital allocation. This increased allocation is essential for acquiring major systems (air defence radar, maritime surveillance systems, anti-submarine detection systems) required for LOA 2.

### Operations and Maintenance

Approximately 12 per cent of the defence budget (approximately €150 million) goes to operations and maintenance of existing equipment and facilities.

### Structural Constraints on Budget Composition

The current composition of Irish defence spending reflects structural constraints:

**Historical Equipment**: The Irish Defence Forces operate equipment acquired in the 1970s and 1980s. This equipment requires substantial maintenance and support. Modern equipment requires less maintenance (typically 8-12% of purchase cost annually vs. 15-20% for older equipment), meaning that replacement with modern systems can eventually reduce operations and maintenance costs.

**Personnel Costs**: Irish defence salaries and benefits are competitive with private sector in Ireland, creating wage pressure. However, reducing personnel costs through lower salaries would create recruitment and retention challenges.

**Facility Requirements**: Ireland's maritime geography and the requirement for national presence across the country requires maintaining facilities in multiple locations, creating facility costs.

### Comparison with Other Nations

Other European nations allocate defence budgets differently:

- **Major powers**: Germany, France spend more heavily on capital (40-50 per cent of budget) due to larger economies and ability to invest in major systems.
- **Smaller NATO members**: Often allocate 30-40 per cent to capital, 50-55 per cent to personnel, remainder to operations.
- **Transition nations**: Poland and other states increasing defence spending often front-load capital (40-50 per cent) to rapidly acquire capability, accepting temporary reduction in personnel spending.

Ireland's current composition (58 per cent personnel, 30 per cent capital) is not unusual for smaller states but reflects the constraint that with limited total budget, achieving higher capital spending requires reducing other categories.

## The €1.7 Billion Capital Plan: Assessment of Adequacy

The government's commitment to a €1.7 billion capital plan for 2026-2030 (approximately €340 million annually) represents the most significant increase in defence capital spending in decades. This plan is essential for acquiring major systems required for LOA 2. However, assessing the adequacy of this investment requires understanding what capabilities it will purchase and what timelines are required.

### Major Systems Planned or Required

The primary systems required for LOA 2 and LOA 3 transition include:

**Air Defence Radar System**: A comprehensive, multi-layer air defence radar system providing detection and tracking of aircraft across Irish airspace. Cost estimate: €400-600 million. Timeline: 3-5 years from contract to operational capability.

**Maritime Surveillance System**: Integrated system combining surface radar, underwater detection, and satellite integration for comprehensive maritime domain awareness. Cost estimate: €200-350 million. Timeline: 4-6 years.

**Anti-Submarine Warfare Capability**: Systems for detection and engagement of submarine threats. Cost estimates: €150-300 million (depending on whether manned or unmanned systems). Timeline: 5-7 years.

**Naval Vessel Modernization/Replacement**: The Oileán Gheálach-class vessels require replacement by mid-2030s. Estimated cost for replacement: €500 million-€1 billion. Multiple vessels required (3-4), timeline: 10+ years.

**Air Mobility**: Transport aircraft replacement or upgrade. Estimated cost: €300-500 million. Timeline: 5-10 years.

**Ammunition and Weapons**: Anti-ship missiles, air-to-air missiles, small arms ammunition. Estimated cost: €200-400 million (phased over multiple years).

**Facilities and Infrastructure**: Facility modernization, communications infrastructure, secure operations centers. Estimated cost: €300-500 million.

**Total Estimated Requirement for LOA 2**: Approximately €2.5-3.5 billion in capital expenditure over 5-7 years.

**Total Estimated Requirement for LOA 3**: Approximately €5-7 billion in capital expenditure over 10-15 years.

### Adequacy Assessment

The €1.7 billion capital plan for 2026-2030 covers approximately 50-65 per cent of estimated requirements for LOA 2. This plan would allow acquisition of one or two major systems (e.g., air defence radar and maritime surveillance) but would defer others (e.g., naval vessel replacement, air mobility).

For LOA 3, achieving full capability would require sustained capital spending of €500-700 million annually through the 2030s, plus replacement of major systems acquired in the 2020s as they age. This represents commitment to sustained elevated spending through the coming decades.

### Timing Challenges

A critical challenge is timing of capital deployment. Major systems require 3-7 years from contract to operational capability. The €1.7 billion plan must be deployed in sequence that matches these timelines. If capital is deployed in suboptimal sequence (e.g., acquiring ground support equipment before the systems they support), capability development will be delayed.

The Ireland Defence Procurement Authority and strategic planning establishment must carefully sequence the capital programme to optimize capability development timelines.

## Personnel Requirements and Growth Planning

Achieving LOA 2 and LOA 3 requires personnel growth beyond current 9,000 personnel strength:

**LOA 2 Requirement**: Approximately 10,000-10,500 personnel. This represents growth of 1,000-1,500 personnel, or roughly 11-17 per cent increase. Over a 5-year period (2026-2030), this is achievable at approximately 200-300 recruits annually above natural replacement requirements.

**LOA 3 Requirement**: Approximately 13,000 personnel. This represents growth of approximately 4,000 personnel from current levels, or 44 per cent increase. Over a 10-year period, this is achievable at approximately 400 recruits annually above natural replacement requirements.

Personnel growth requires:

**Recruitment**: Expanding recruitment and training capacity. Current recruitment is adequate for replacement but insufficient for growth. Recruitment campaigns, improved salaries and benefits (if necessary), and expanded training capacity are required.

**Training Infrastructure**: Officer training capacity requires expansion. Current Irish Defence Forces officer training academy has capacity for approximately 80-100 commissions annually. LOA 3 requires capacity for 150+ commissions annually.

**Career Development**: Creating attractive career pathways in new specializations (air defence, cyber, advanced maritime operations) to attract and retain qualified personnel.

**Retention**: Addressing retention challenges in critical specializations through improved pay, career pathways, and professional development opportunities.

**Support Infrastructure**: Personnel growth requires support infrastructure (accommodation, facilities, logistics). This requires capital investment beyond weapon systems.

The personnel costs of growing to LOA 3 are approximately €120 million annually (additional 4,000 personnel at €30,000 per-soldier cost for salary and benefits). This represents increase from current €720 million to approximately €840 million for personnel, remaining at 58-60 per cent of an enlarged total defence budget.

## Funding Requirements for Transition to LOA 3

### Scenario Analysis

To provide context for understanding funding requirements, this section presents scenario analysis for different pathways to LOA 3:

**Scenario A: Moderate Growth (Current Trajectory)**

- Annual defence spending grows from €1.24 billion (2025) to €2.0 billion by 2030, then to €2.5 billion by 2035
- Capital allocation increases from 30 per cent to 40 per cent of budget as percentage of personnel costs decline
- Personnel grows to 10,500 by 2028 (LOA 2), then to 13,000 by 2035 (LOA 3)
- Timeline to LOA 3: 2035-2038

**Scenario B: Accelerated Growth (2% of GDP target)**

- Annual defence spending reaches approximately 2.0 per cent of GDP by 2030 (approximately €2.4 billion based on projected GDP), maintains that level
- Capital allocation reaches 45 per cent of budget by 2030
- Personnel grows to 10,500 by 2028, 13,000 by 2032
- Timeline to LOA 3: 2032-2035

**Scenario C: Aggressive Growth (2.5% of GDP target)**

- Annual defence spending reaches 2.5 per cent of GDP by 2030 (approximately €3.0 billion), sustained through 2035
- Capital allocation reaches 50 per cent of budget by 2030
- Personnel grows to 10,500 by 2027, 13,000 by 2031
- Timeline to LOA 3: 2031-2034

### Cumulative Spending Requirements

Total cumulative defence spending required to achieve LOA 3 by different timelines:

- **Scenario A (LOA 3 by 2038)**: Approximately €25-28 billion cumulative spending over 13 years (average €1.9-2.1 billion annually)
- **Scenario B (LOA 3 by 2035)**: Approximately €27-30 billion cumulative spending over 10 years (average €2.7-3.0 billion annually)
- **Scenario C (LOA 3 by 2034)**: Approximately €29-32 billion cumulative spending over 9 years (average €3.2-3.5 billion annually)

### Financial Feasibility Assessment

All three scenarios are financially feasible for Ireland. Ireland's annual GDP is approximately €600+ billion, and government annual spending is approximately €100-110 billion. Defence spending of €2.0-3.0 billion annually represents approximately 2-3 per cent of government spending, or 0.3-0.5 per cent of GDP. This is readily affordable compared to government budgets and GDP.

The Irish government currently runs significant budget surpluses (€4-6 billion annually in recent years). A single year's budget surplus would be sufficient to fully fund the capital requirements of LOA 3 modernization. The constraint on Irish defence development is not financial but institutional—the capacity of government and defence forces to plan and execute spending efficiently.

## Policy Recommendations

Based on this analysis of Irish defence spending, several policy recommendations emerge:

### 1. Sustain Increased Spending Commitment

The government's commitment to increased defence spending (€1.5 billion in Budget 2026, €1.7 billion capital plan 2026-2030) should be sustained and, if possible, increased. This commitment should be multi-year (10+ years) to enable long-term planning by defence forces and defence industries.

### 2. Front-Load Capital Spending

The allocation of capital spending in the €1.7 billion plan should prioritize major systems (air defence radar, maritime surveillance) in early years to enable timely capability development.

### 3. Expand Personnel Training and Development

Investment in officer training expansion, non-commissioned officer training, and specialist training should be prioritized to enable personnel growth required for LOA 2 and LOA 3.

### 4. Develop Defence Industrial Strategy

As defence spending increases, industrial benefits can be maximized through explicit defence industrial strategy that directs spending toward Irish firms where possible and builds Irish defence industry capacity.

### 5. Secure European Partnership Support

Through PESCO and other EU mechanisms, Ireland should seek partnerships that supplement national spending with European co-investment in capability development.

### 6. Improve Spending Efficiency

Procurement system modernization and improved project management can increase the efficiency with which defence spending is converted into capability.

## Conclusion

Ireland's defence spending has historically been among the lowest in Europe, reflecting historical policy choices and post-Cold War assumptions. Recent increases represent necessary adjustment to changed strategic environment. The €1.7 billion capital plan represents significant commitment, but the adequacy of current and projected spending depends on long-term trajectory.

Achieving LOA 2 by 2028 requires sustaining current spending increases. Achieving LOA 3 requires sustained spending growth through the 2030s, likely reaching 1.5-2.0 per cent of GDP by the 2030s. This is financially feasible and represents modest increase compared to peer nations and international benchmarks.

The binding constraint on Irish defence development is not financial but institutional—the capacity to plan large-scale modernization, to manage complex procurements, and to implement sustained capability development.
