## The Scale and Composition of Irish Defence Spending

Ireland's defence budget of €1.24 billion in 2026 represents a significant commitment in absolute terms but translates to just 0.22% of GDP—the lowest defence spending ratio in the European Union. This figure requires contextualisation: absolute budget size tells one story, budget intensity (as a share of national output) tells another, and the composition of that spending tells a third.

Absolute budget size matters for purchasing power. A €1.24 billion defence budget can acquire meaningful equipment: patrol vessels, surveillance systems, transport aircraft, or ammunition stocks. However, comparing defence spending as a share of GDP reveals institutional prioritisation. Ireland spends 0.22% of GDP on defence; the European median is approximately 1.5–1.8%; NATO members commit to a minimum of 2%. This disparity reflects political choice, not fiscal incapacity. Ireland's current fiscal surplus exceeds 2% of GDP annually. From a pure fiscal perspective, funding a substantially larger defence budget is feasible.

The composition of Irish defence spending reveals the constraint beneath the headline number. Of the €1.24 billion annual budget:

- **Personnel costs: 58%** (approximately €720 million annually). This covers salaries, allowances, pension liabilities, and training for Defence Force personnel. With a Defence Force establishment of approximately 7,300 personnel, this represents an average cost of approximately €98,000 per uniformed staff member, including institutional overheads and support staff.

- **Operations and maintenance: 14%** (approximately €174 million). This covers fuel, consumables, bases, and routine maintenance of existing equipment.

- **Capital equipment: 28%** (approximately €350 million). This is new procurement: acquisitions of ships, aircraft, communications systems, and weapons.

The personnel-intensive structure reflects historical decisions. The Defence Force was sized during the Cold War for territorial defence with limited expeditionary capability. That manning structure persists: the vast majority of budget goes to paying the existing force, leaving limited capacity for equipment modernisation.

## Budget Trajectory and Growth Rates

Irish defence spending has grown consistently since 2018, but from a low baseline. In 2018, the defence budget was €830 million. By 2026, it has reached €1.24 billion—a 50% increase in absolute terms over eight years. However, this growth conceals a complex trajectory.

The growth rate has accelerated. Between 2018 and 2023, defence spending increased at an average rate of 3.2% annually. In 2024–2025, the rate increased to 5.1%. In 2026, growth reached 6.2%. This acceleration reflects political momentum following Russia's invasion of Ukraine and the government's commitment to Levels of Ambition (LOA) defence capability targets.

However, the growth rate remains insufficient to close the capability gap. A 6.2% annual increase, if sustained, would double the defence budget in approximately 12 years. Peer nations—Poland, the Baltics, France—are growing defence budgets at similar or faster rates from significantly larger baselines. Poland's defence budget in 2026 is €15 billion; it is growing at 8% annually. France's budget is €50 billion, growing at 4% annually. Ireland's growth is real but occurs within a framework of persistent under-investment compared to peer nations.

## International Perspective: Where Ireland Sits

Ireland's 0.22% of GDP defence spending places it at the lowest end of the European distribution. The comparison reveals structural differences in defence investment priorities across the EU.

The Baltics and Poland prioritise defence spending at 2.5–3.5% of GDP, reflecting exposure to Russian military capabilities and NATO membership. France and Germany allocate 1.9–2.0% of GDP to defence. Smaller EU members like Belgium, Czech Republic, and Slovakia allocate 1.3–1.6%. Ireland stands alone at 0.22%.

![Alt text](/images/charts/ids_gdp_comparison.png)

The outlier position is not new, but it has become politically contested. The Strategic Compass process and the government's commitment to Levels of Ambition defence capability have created implicit pressure to increase defence spending ratios. However, there is no EU mandate or NATO requirement for Ireland specifically. Ireland is not a NATO member and operates under EU defence frameworks, which do not specify minimum defence spending ratios. The pressure is political and strategic, not institutional.

## The Capital Plan and Procurement Demand

A critical shift is underway. In May 2024, the government approved a €1.7 billion capital expenditure plan for defence (2026–2030). This is not additional budget but a reallocation within the defence envelope toward equipment modernisation. In practice, this means defence spending composition will shift. The share devoted to capital equipment will increase from the current 28% toward 35–40% by 2030, while the personnel share remains relatively stable.

The capital plan creates procurement demand for the first time in a decade. It includes: acquisition of maritime surveillance vessels, modernisation of the air transport fleet, investment in coastal surveillance systems, cyber infrastructure, and logistics capacity. No single item dominates the capital plan, reflecting the breadth of capability gaps across the Defence Force. This is deliberate policy: spreading procurement across multiple small-to-medium projects ensures technological diversification, reduces single-point-of-failure risks, and provides opportunities for Irish and European industrial participation.

The €1.7 billion capital allocation over five years implies an average capital budget of €340 million annually—approximately equivalent to current capital spending. This does not increase the total defence budget but redistributes its composition, creating headroom for equipment modernisation while maintaining personnel levels. The political constraint that has historically prevented capital spending—the competition with personnel costs for scarce budget—has been addressed not through larger budgets but through budget reallocation.

## Funding Sources and Fiscal Space

Where does the €1.24 billion annual defence budget come from? Like all government spending, it comes from general taxation. Ireland's headline corporate tax rate is 12.5%, and personal income tax yields approximately €22 billion annually. Defence spending, at €1.24 billion, represents approximately 5.6% of total tax revenue—not the largest allocation (healthcare and education consume larger shares) but a material percentage of the fiscal budget.

More significantly, Ireland maintains a structural fiscal surplus. Budget surpluses in 2023 and 2024 exceeded €2 billion annually. Projections for 2025–2026 suggest surpluses will continue, though at lower levels (approximately €1–1.5 billion annually) due to planned spending increases in healthcare and education. These surpluses create fiscal space. From a budgetary perspective, increasing defence spending from 0.22% of GDP to 0.5% of GDP would cost approximately €1.5 billion annually—entirely absorbable within existing or projected fiscal surpluses.

This matters for the Levels of Ambition transition. Moving from LOA 1 to LOA 2 (current commitment, achieving by 2028) requires capital spending of approximately €1.7 billion over five years—already planned. Moving from LOA 2 to LOA 3 (self-defence capability) would require additional capital spending of approximately €3–4 billion over a decade. The government has stated that LOA 3 would be funded from a single year's fiscal surplus if politically decided. The constraint on defence transformation is not money; it is institutional bandwidth.

## Institutional Bandwidth: The True Constraint

The €1.24 billion defence budget masks a deeper constraint: the ability of Irish defence and security institutions to absorb and deploy capital efficiently. This constraint has three dimensions: procurement system sophistication, security clearance infrastructure, and industrial participation policy.

### Procurement System Sophistication

Ireland's defence procurement system is competent but not sophisticated. It can acquire equipment through established processes: requisition, competitive tendering, contract award, and delivery. However, it lacks the specialist capability to manage complex, multi-year development programmes. European nations procuring cutting-edge defence systems establish dedicated procurement teams, partner with industry on technology roadmaps, and manage technical risk through phased development. Ireland's Defence Forces Procurement Office operates with a staff of approximately 15–20 people managing the entire defence equipment acquisition portfolio.

This is not criticism of individual competence but recognition of scale. A €1.7 billion capital plan over five years requires specialist procurement capacity that does not currently exist in dedicated form. The Department of Defence and the Defence Forces are building this capacity, but it takes 18–24 months to recruit, clear, and train specialist procurement personnel.

### Security Clearance Infrastructure

Irish firms cannot participate in classified EU defence programmes because Ireland has no security clearance system for personnel or facilities. Every European defence-procuring nation has a security clearance process: personnel undergo vetting, facilities are assessed for security compliance, and classified information is compartmentalised according to standardised protocols.

Ireland has no equivalent system. Irish citizens can hold UK, EU, or NATO security clearances, but Ireland itself does not issue them. This creates a structural barrier: an Irish technology firm developing maritime surveillance software cannot bid for classified contracts with the Irish Defence Forces because the firm's personnel lack security clearance, and the firm cannot legally handle classified information.

The government has moved to address this through interim measures. In 2024, the Department of Defence implemented an interim security clearance process for industrial participation in EDF and EDIP programmes. However, this is a temporary solution. A permanent security clearance infrastructure requires legislation (the Security and Intelligence Act, currently in development), institutional capacity (a vetting authority with statutory powers), and integration with EU and NATO clearance reciprocity frameworks. This infrastructure is being built but will not be operationally ready until late 2027 at the earliest.

### Industrial Participation Policy

Ireland lacks an explicit industrial participation policy for defence procurement. When a government agency procures equipment, European governments typically stipulate that a percentage of the contract value must be subcontracted to domestic suppliers or participate in technology transfer agreements. This is industrial policy embedded within procurement: it grows domestic industrial capacity while acquiring equipment.

Ireland has no such policy. When a defence procurement occurs, the contract is awarded to the lowest qualified bidder regardless of national origin. This is economically rational if the objective is pure cost minimisation. However, it forgoes the opportunity to develop domestic industrial capacity or embed Irish firms in European defence supply chains.

The government is beginning to address this through the Defence Industrial Policy Framework (DIPF), still in development as of March 2026. The framework will establish principles for industrial participation in future defence procurement, with target ranges for domestic participation (typically 15–30% of contract value, typical across Europe). However, formalising this policy requires legislative change and EU coordination. It will not be operational until 2027.

## Fiscal Capacity Versus Institutional Readiness

The crucial insight emerges from this analysis: Ireland could fund substantially larger defence budgets immediately. A €2 billion annual defence budget (0.35% of GDP) would be fiscally feasible. A €2.5 billion budget (0.43% of GDP) would strain public finances marginally but remain feasible. Yet the Defence Forces cannot meaningfully absorb capital at these scales within the current institutional framework.

The constraint is not money. It is institutional readiness: the procurement capacity to manage larger capital programmes, the security clearance infrastructure to partner with industry on classified programmes, and the industrial participation policy to ensure capital spending catalyses domestic industrial development rather than simply funding imports.

The government's approach—gradually increasing the defence budget while simultaneously building institutional infrastructure—is therefore rational. The 6.2% annual growth rate reflects realistic capacity to absorb capital. Doubling defence spending overnight would create waste: funds would be spent inefficiently because the institutional systems to deploy them productively do not yet exist.

## Strategic Implications

Ireland's low defence spending reflects historical factors: geographic remoteness from traditional threat axes, EU and NATO collective defence frameworks, and domestic political constraints on military spending. These factors persist, but the strategic environment has shifted. Russian aggression in Ukraine has created immediate security challenges. Hybrid threats—disinformation, cyber attacks, critical infrastructure targeting—pose year-round risks. The security landscape of 2026 is qualitatively different from 2018.

The budget response—6.2% annual growth, capital plan allocation, institutional infrastructure development—is proportionate to this changed environment. It signals that Ireland takes security seriously without suggesting Irish threat perception has converged with Polish or Baltic perspectives. It reflects EU and transatlantic security commitments without implying NATO membership.

The trajectory is important. If current growth rates are sustained, Irish defence spending will reach 0.35% of GDP by 2030. This would place Ireland in the lower-middle range of European defence spenders, above only the smallest EU members. Whether the political will exists to sustain this trajectory beyond 2030 remains an open question. However, the institutional foundations being built now will determine whether that increased spending is deployed effectively or wasted through inefficient procurement.

The fiscal capacity exists. The political decision is underway. The institutional readiness is being built. The outcome depends on whether the government can sustain commitment through the inevitable political cycles and competing budget priorities that lie ahead.
