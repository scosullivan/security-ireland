## Where the Money Comes From

Ireland's defence and security funding flows through five distinct channels. Understanding which channel funds what explains why the system is configured the way it is — and where the untapped potential lies.

### 1. The National Defence Budget

This is the core. Ireland's defence vote covers personnel, operations, and capital. The capital programme — €1.7 billion over 2026–2030 — is the transformative element. Historically, Ireland spent almost nothing on defence equipment. The current programme represents a step-change.

Even at the enhanced level, Ireland's total defence investment is a rounding error by European standards. Germany's debt brake reform alone unlocked €400 billion. The SAFE instrument offers €150 billion in loans. Ireland's entire five-year capital programme is less than 1% of either figure.

What the budget funds: personnel costs for 7,531 permanent Defence Forces (against a 9,739 establishment); the primary radar programme (targeting 2028); counter-drone procurement (summer 2026); fleet renewal; vehicle replacement.

What it cannot fund: the institutional transformation required to absorb the equipment. A procurement system designed for routine replacement is now running first-generation capability builds.

### 2. EU Instruments

**PESCO:** Ireland participates in 7 projects as a full member: Maritime Surveillance Upgrade, Maritime Mine Countermeasures, Critical Seabed Infrastructure Protection, Cyber Threats and Incident Response Information Sharing, Cyber Ranges Federation, Military Mobility, and Upgrade of Maritime Surveillance.

**SAFE (€150bn loans):** Ireland is eligible for SAFE loans for maritime and cyber procurement. Preferential terms for joint procurement could reduce costs. Phase 2 terms, negotiated during Ireland's Presidency, will determine access conditions.

**EDIP (€1.5bn grants):** Implementing rules being written during Ireland's Presidency determine whether 'defence industrial participation' includes technology and dual-use contributions — or only traditional military production.

**EDF (€8bn R&D):** Collaborative research in cyber, AI, and maritime sensing. Feeds future procurement pipelines.

### 3. Bilateral UK–Ireland Cooperation

The Cork Summit of March 2026 operationalised bilateral security cooperation for the first time. The refreshed MoU covers maritime cooperation, cyber defence, air domain information sharing, and subsea infrastructure protection.

### 4. NATO-Adjacent Frameworks

Ireland joined NATO's Individually Tailored Partnership Programme in 2024 and applied in March 2026 to the North Sea Joint Declaration on subsea security. These provide access to standards, exercises, and intelligence-sharing without alliance membership.

### 5. CISE Maritime Data Infrastructure

Ireland joined CISE in April 2025. Access to maritime surveillance data partially compensates for the Naval Service's inability to physically patrol its 880,000 km² EEZ.

## Where the Money Goes

Ireland's defence and security ambitions span seven domains. Each is at a different stage, faces different constraints, and draws on different funding channels.

**Airspace:** No primary radar, no fighters, no surface-to-air missiles. Primary radar programme targeting 2028 — Ireland's first-ever air surveillance. Counter-drone for summer 2026.

**Maritime:** Nine vessels, no functioning armament, one large vessel deployable. 880,000 km² of EEZ with less than 15% surveillance coverage. Fleet renewal in capital programme; PESCO projects; CISE data layer.

**Subsea Infrastructure:** 20+ major cables making landfall in Ireland carrying 90% of transatlantic data. €1 billion EU Cable Action Plan. Ireland's highest-leverage domain.

**Cyber & Hybrid Resilience:** Ireland hosts EU HQs of most major American tech companies. 2021 HSE ransomware attack cost hundreds of millions.

**Intelligence Architecture:** Split across three agencies with no unified national security structure. Unique in Europe.

**Personnel:** 7,531 against 9,739 establishment — a fifty-year low. Every capability domain requires people.

**Defence Industrial Base:** Ireland has no defence industrial base. But the €1.7 billion capital programme creates procurement demand for the first time.

## The Three Cooperation Tracks

Ireland's security architecture operates through three tracks that feed into each other. EU frameworks (PESCO, CISE, EDIP/SAFE eligibility) provide institutional legitimacy and funding access. Bilateral UK–Ireland cooperation (Cork MoU, Subsea Framework, exercises) builds operational capacity. NATO-adjacent frameworks (ITPP, North Sea Declaration) connect Ireland to alliance standards without membership.

## What's Missing

No National Security Strategy — Ireland remains one of few EU states without one. No LOA transition roadmap — 54 of 130 Commission recommendations completed in four years, rate slowing. No permanent financing architecture — unlike Germany's constitutional reform, Ireland's programme can be reversed.
