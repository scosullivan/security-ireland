On 9 April 2026, the Minister for Justice deployed the Defence Forces against civilian fuel protesters using a legal framework that has not been substantially updated since 1954. The Defence Act provides the authority. What it does not provide is any constraint on how that authority is exercised: no proportionality test, no time limit, no parliamentary notification, no rules of engagement for domestic operations, no independent oversight, and no property damage liability framework. This proposal establishes seven requirements that should govern every future domestic military deployment.

## The Problem

The Defence Act 1954 gives the government authority to deploy the Defence Forces in aid to the civil power when requested by An Garda Síochána via the C70 process. This authority is necessary. But it is also unlimited: no statutory proportionality test, no defined escalation sequence, no time limitation, no parliamentary review, no published rules of engagement for domestic operations, no independent civilian oversight, and no property damage framework. The Minister's statement that vehicle owners 'should not complain later about any damage caused' illustrates the gap between the authority the state claims and the accountability framework it has built. Every comparable EU member state has proportionality requirements for domestic military deployment. Ireland has none.

## The Proposed Framework

The following seven requirements should be enacted as amendments to the Defence Act 1954, or as standalone legislation governing military aid to the civil power in domestic civil disorder situations.

![The proposed seven-gate process](/images/charts/pf_seven_gates.png)

### 1. The Necessity Test

Before a C70 form is issued for a civil disorder situation, the Garda Commissioner must certify in writing that all available Garda resources have been deployed or assessed and are insufficient. The certification must confirm that mutual aid from other Garda divisions, the Public Order Unit, the Armed Support Unit, the Emergency Response Unit, and commercial alternatives (such as heavy vehicle removal) have been considered and are inadequate. Military deployment must be demonstrably the last resort, not the first option.

### 2. The Proportionality Test

The C70 request must specify the nature of the situation, the specific military capability required, and why that capability is proportionate to the threat or disruption. The request cannot be for 'the Defence Forces' in general. It must specify a defined capability for a defined task: heavy vehicle removal capability, engineering support, logistics, or personnel. The military capability deployed must be the minimum necessary to achieve the specific objective.

### 3. The Civilian Character Distinction

The framework must formally distinguish between security threats and civil disorder. For civil disorder — protest, industrial action, infrastructure blockade by civilians — the threshold for military deployment must be higher than for security threats. Military deployment is authorised only when the situation poses an immediate, demonstrable risk to life, to the continuous operation of essential services, or to critical national infrastructure. Traffic disruption, economic inconvenience, public transport delays, political embarrassment, or disruption to commercial activity do not meet the threshold.

### 4. Time Limitation and Renewal

Every domestic military deployment under aid to the civil power must be time-limited. The initial authorisation must specify a maximum duration of 72 hours. If the deployment is required beyond 72 hours, the Minister must issue a written renewal with a fresh proportionality assessment. Each renewal is limited to a further 72 hours. There is no provision for indefinite deployment. If no renewal is issued, the deployment authority lapses automatically.

### 5. Parliamentary Notification and Review

The Dáil must be notified within 24 hours of any domestic military deployment under aid to the civil power. Within 7 sitting days, the responsible Minister must present a written report to the Oireachtas Committee on Defence and National Security covering the legal basis, necessity certification, proportionality assessment, scope, duration, and any incidents. The Committee has the power to summon the Garda Commissioner, the Chief of Staff, and the relevant Minister to give evidence.

### 6. Rules of Engagement and Property Damage

Published rules of engagement must exist for domestic aid to the civil power operations involving civil disorder. The ROE must address the level of physical force authorised for moving persons and vehicles, whether restraint is authorised, the procedures for handling resistance, the chain of command for escalation decisions, and the documentation requirements. The state is liable for disproportionate damage to private property, and a pre-agreed liability framework must include a claims process, an independent assessment mechanism, and a compensation timeline.

### 7. Independent Oversight

An independent civilian body must have jurisdiction to investigate complaints arising from military conduct during domestic aid to the civil power operations. This function could be assigned to GSOC with expanded jurisdiction, to the Ombudsman for the Defence Forces with expanded powers, or to a new independent body. The oversight body must have the power to investigate use of force complaints, property damage complaints, proportionality of the deployment itself, compliance with the rules of engagement, and the adequacy of the necessity certification.

## How This Framework Would Have Applied on 9 April 2026

Under this framework, the deployment of 9 April would have required the Garda Commissioner to certify that all Garda resources were insufficient. The C70 request would have had to specify the exact military capability needed and why it was proportionate. The deployment would have been time-limited to 72 hours with mandatory Dáil notification within 24 hours. Published rules of engagement would have governed soldier conduct. And an independent body would have jurisdiction over any complaints. At least three of the seven gates would not have been passed under the conditions of 9 April.

## Implementation

This framework can be implemented through three routes, in order of preference. First, primary legislation amending the Defence Act 1954 provides the strongest legal foundation and creates enforceable rights. Second, a ministerial directive from the Minister for Defence could establish administrative proportionality requirements as an interim measure. Third, an Oireachtas Committee recommendation could create political pressure for legislative action.
