European human rights law distinguishes four levels of protest activity, each with a different legal status and a different proportionate response. Ireland's government has collapsed all four levels into one — treating peaceful rallies, civil disobedience, infrastructure obstruction, and violence as a single category and responding with the most extreme tools available.

![The Protest Spectrum](/images/charts/ps_protest_spectrum.png)
*The protest spectrum: European human rights law distinguishes four levels of protest activity. Each has a different legal status and requires a different proportionate response. Ireland treated the entire spectrum as Level 4 and responded with military deployment, pepper spray, and threatening rhetoric.*

## What the law actually says

The right to peaceful assembly is fundamental. Article 11 of the European Convention on Human Rights and Article 12 of the EU Charter of Fundamental Rights protect the right to peaceful assembly. This is not a concession from the state — it is a fundamental right that the state is obligated to protect and facilitate.

Civil disobedience is protected. The European Parliament's 2019 Resolution on the Right to Peaceful Protest explicitly recognises that peaceful civil disobedience is protected under the right of peaceful assembly. Road blockades, sit-ins, and slow-moving convoys are disruptive. They are not violent. They are protected.

Infrastructure obstruction is more complex but still subject to proportionality. Any restriction must satisfy three tests under European Court of Human Rights case law: legality, necessity, and proportionality. Deploying the military is not the least restrictive means when negotiation, court injunctions, and specialist police units have not been tried.

Even criminal conduct requires proportionate response. Penalties must not be used to silence dissent or deter individuals from exercising their right to protest.

## What Ireland did wrong

No distinction between protest levels. The government treated peaceful rallies on O'Connell Street, tractor convoys on the M50, and fuel depot blockades at Whitegate as a single phenomenon requiring a single response.

Escalation without graduated response. Ireland moved directly from routine policing to military deployment and pepper spray without exhausting intermediate options. The government skipped every intermediate step because the institutional architecture for intermediate steps does not exist.

Language designed to criminalise rather than de-escalate. "National sabotage." "Illegal activity endangering the State." This language treats economic protesters as enemies of the state rather than citizens exercising their fundamental rights.

No negotiation. The refusal to engage directly with the people actually blockading infrastructure — while simultaneously deploying military force against them — is the response of a state with no institutional channel for managing unstructured grievance.

## The cost of getting it wrong

The government's disproportionate response has not resolved the crisis — it has transformed it into something far more dangerous: far-right capture, a legitimacy crisis, international exposure three months before the EU Presidency, and a precedent for future military deployment against civilian protest.
